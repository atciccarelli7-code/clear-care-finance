# Diagnosis, Explained — Seven-Guide Second Review

**Review date:** July 22, 2026  
**Guides reviewed:** Acute Kidney Injury, Atrial Fibrillation, Gastrointestinal Bleeding, Bowel Obstruction, High Blood Pressure, High Cholesterol, and Kidney Failure  
**Internal comparators:** Heart Failure, Explained and COPD, Explained  
**External comparators:** Current Mayo Clinic, WebMD, MedlinePlus, NIDDK, American Heart Association, National Kidney Foundation, and condition-specific professional sources.

## Decision

The seven guides are sufficiently complete for qualified independent clinical review and observed patient/caregiver usability testing. They are **not clinically final** and remain noindex, nofollow, ad-free, and excluded from the sitemap.

The expansion works as one product system rather than seven unrelated articles. Each guide gives the reader:

1. a 30-second explanation;
2. common misconceptions;
3. clinically meaningful patterns;
4. the question each test is trying to answer;
5. treatment goals before treatment names;
6. purpose-first medication cards;
7. procedures and supportive care;
8. a daily operating plan;
9. emergency, prompt-contact, and stable-day action levels;
10. care-team questions;
11. teach-back; and
12. a concise printable handout.

## Comparative strengths

### Against Mayo Clinic

Mayo remains the strongest general-coverage benchmark. Its condition pages usually provide broad diagnosis, testing, treatment, procedure, lifestyle, and appointment-preparation coverage. Community Acquired Finance now provides comparable topic breadth while offering a clearer first-use path:

- understand the diagnosis;
- understand the medicines;
- know what to do each day; and
- know when to get help.

CAF also separates emergency action from same-day contact more consistently and puts medication boundaries next to the medication rather than only in a general disclaimer.

### Against WebMD

WebMD often provides current medical-review dates and useful single-topic medication articles. Its information is frequently fragmented across an overview, treatment article, medicine article, and procedure article. CAF keeps the patient’s full mental model, operating plan, and discharge questions in one governed guide.

CAF must not imitate WebMD’s search-fragmentation model. Depth should remain inside a coherent guide, with advanced cards collapsed rather than split into overlapping pages for search volume.

### Against MedlinePlus, NIDDK, AHA, and NKF

Federal and specialty patient resources remain the preferred plain-language authorities. They are concise and trustworthy, but often do not provide CAF’s medication-purpose cards, barrier checks, restart-owner questions, three-level action plans, or discharge teach-back.

CAF’s role is not to replace these sources. It is to organize their accurate content into a nurse-led learning and action system.

## Condition-by-condition findings

### Acute kidney injury

**What worked**

- Clearly distinguishes AKI from permanent kidney failure.
- Explains why creatinine trend, urine output, electrolytes, and the clinical situation matter more than one isolated number.
- Separates reduced perfusion, direct tissue injury, and obstruction without diagnosing the reader’s cause.
- Prevents the unsafe universal advice to drink more fluid.
- Requires a medicine hold/adjust/restart owner.
- Explains that dialysis is not triggered by one creatinine threshold.

**Correction confirmed during review**

The guide explicitly states that KDIGO’s 2026 AKI/AKD document is a public-review draft and that the 2012 AKI guideline remains the current final KDIGO guideline until a final update is published.

**Residual review focus**

A nephrology reviewer should verify fluid-language nuance, medicine-restart language, electrolyte escalation, and post-AKI follow-up timing.

### Atrial fibrillation

**What worked**

- Organizes care into three separate jobs: stroke prevention, rate control, and rhythm control.
- Explains paroxysmal, persistent, long-standing persistent, and permanent AFib without implying that intermittent AFib removes stroke risk.
- States that aspirin is not a universal substitute when anticoagulation is indicated.
- Protects against independent anticoagulant interruption for bruising, a normal-feeling rhythm, dental work, surgery, or endoscopy.
- Covers cardioversion, ablation, left atrial appendage occlusion, and AV-node ablation without assigning candidacy.

**Comparator finding retained as an advanced-care boundary**

The 2023 ACC/AHA/ACCP/HRS guideline generally prefers direct oral anticoagulants over warfarin for eligible patients, with important valve-related exceptions. The guide asks why the exact anticoagulant fits the patient’s valve status, kidney/liver function, interactions, access, and bleeding context rather than turning that exception into a self-selection rule.

**Residual review focus**

Cardiology or electrophysiology review should verify cardioversion anticoagulation language, antiarrhythmic monitoring, left atrial appendage occlusion boundaries, and emergency rate-language.

### Gastrointestinal bleeding

**What worked**

- Presents GI bleeding as a sign from another condition rather than one disease.
- Separates upper, small-bowel/lower, and acute/chronic patterns.
- Explains why stool color does not definitively locate or measure a bleed.
- Explains hemoglobin lag and avoids one universal transfusion threshold.
- Separates CT angiography, endoscopy, colonoscopy, angiography, and capsule/enteroscopy by clinical question.
- Treats antithrombotic interruption and restart as a two-risk decision with a named owner.

**Comparator finding**

General health sites often present the medication list as a cause list. CAF more accurately distinguishes the lesion that bled from a medicine that may increase bleeding severity or complicate treatment.

**Residual review focus**

GI review should verify transfusion, reversal, endoscopy-timing, variceal, H. pylori, and recurrence language.

### Bowel obstruction

**What worked**

- Distinguishes mechanical obstruction from ileus or pseudo-obstruction.
- Separates partial/complete obstruction from strangulation, ischemia, and perforation.
- Explains why selected adhesive obstructions may be managed nonoperatively without implying that every obstruction should wait.
- Clearly prohibits generic laxative, fiber, enema, antidiarrheal, or diet-progression advice.
- Explains NG decompression, contrast challenge, surgery, stenting, and ostomy purpose.

**Source-age finding**

The WSES Bologna guideline is older but remains the current published WSES adhesive-small-bowel-obstruction guideline and continues to be used and evaluated in recent prospective work. The source matrix records this as a refresh trigger rather than presenting its publication year as proof of currentness.

**Residual review focus**

A surgeon should verify ischemia language, CT and contrast language, nonoperative boundaries, diet progression, and ostomy escalation.

### High blood pressure

**What worked**

- Uses the 2025 AHA/ACC guideline rather than the superseded 2017 framework.
- Teaches correct cuff technique and averaged home readings.
- Distinguishes white-coat, masked, secondary, and resistant hypertension.
- States that cuffless wearable readings are not sufficiently reliable for diagnosis or treatment decisions.
- Separates severe hypertension without acute target-organ damage from hypertensive emergency.
- Preserves pregnancy and postpartum hypertension as an obstetric pathway.

**Comparator finding**

Mayo and general pages explain lifestyle and medicine breadth well. CAF adds the missing operational distinction between “record and review,” “contact the team promptly,” and “call 911 now.”

**Residual review focus**

A hypertension clinician should verify target-language, PREVENT use, resistant/secondary workup, severe-reading instructions, and pregnancy boundaries.

### High cholesterol / dyslipidemia

**What worked**

- Uses the March 2026 AHA/ACC multisociety dyslipidemia guideline.
- Explains LDL-C, non-HDL-C, triglycerides, ApoB, lipoprotein(a), CAC, and PREVENT-ASCVD without assigning a personal risk or goal.
- Restores LDL/non-HDL goals in the correct risk context.
- Treats side effects as a structured evaluation rather than a reason to abandon prevention.
- Distinguishes prescription triglyceride treatment from over-the-counter fish-oil supplements.

**Material freshness corrections applied during review**

1. Added FDA’s July 17, 2026 approval of enlicitide (Lipfendra), the first oral PCSK9 inhibitor, with an explicit warning that PCSK9-directed medicines differ in route, indication, outcome evidence, adverse effects, and access.
2. Added FDA’s June 2026 expanded olezarsen (Tryngolza) indication for adults with severe hypertriglyceridemia and the pooled acute-pancreatitis finding, without presenting it as routine therapy for every elevated triglyceride level.
3. Updated emergency language to include possible acute pancreatitis in the appropriate severe-triglyceride context.

**Residual review focus**

Lipidology or preventive-cardiology review should verify goals, pregnancy language, newer drug indications, cardiovascular-outcome distinctions, and familial-disease screening.

### Kidney failure

**What worked**

- Explains ESKD versus the administratively persistent ESRD term.
- Presents four legitimate paths: hemodialysis, peritoneal dialysis, transplant, and comprehensive conservative care.
- States that dialysis initiation is not determined by one eGFR value.
- Uses a life-plan approach to access rather than a blanket fistula-first statement.
- Provides access-specific infection, bleeding, and failure escalation.
- Prevents generic potassium, phosphorus, sodium, protein, fluid, binder, and treatment-day medication instructions.

**Comparator finding**

Mayo provides broad treatment coverage. NIDDK more clearly recognizes conservative management as active care. CAF follows the NIDDK four-choice structure and adds access safety, medication timing, transportation, supply, power, and caregiver-failure planning.

**Residual review focus**

Nephrology and dialysis-nursing review should verify modality language, access safety, treatment interruption, peritoneal-dialysis infection signs, anemia/mineral-bone medicines, and nutrition boundaries.

- Kept the entire product line ad-free and outside the sitemap while clinical review is pending.

## Remaining release gates

1. Named qualified reviewer for every guide or clearly documented review allocation by specialty.
2. Claim-level clinical review of medication, procedure, monitoring, and escalation language.
3. Observed usability testing with patients, caregivers, and discharge nurses.
4. Demonstrated teach-back of diagnosis, treatment purpose, daily plan, contact plan, and emergency signs.
5. Final review of Letter and A4 handouts for page count, clipping, density, and printer usability.
6. Final source-link and source-date validation before indexing.

## Final product judgment

The major advantage is no longer only “plain English.” The guides convert authoritative medical information into a coherent bedside-to-home operating system. That is a defensible differentiation from large general-health publishers, provided Community Acquired Finance preserves its review gates and does not trade coherence for article volume.
