# Medicare/Medicaid Guide: 10-Page Visual Quick Guide Product Spec

Community Acquired Finance  
Product direction for print-first short guide  
Last updated: 2026-07-08

## Product decision

Build two guide products, not one overloaded PDF:

1. **Short visual quick guide** — approximately 10 pages, print-first, highly scannable, QR-linked, designed for first contact with families, patients, caregivers, healthcare workers, and community readers.
2. **Long reference guide** — existing comprehensive version, optional deeper read, linked from the short guide and site hub.

The short version should be the primary distributed handout. The long version should remain available for readers who want the full explanation, worksheets, endnotes, and expanded source context.

## Why this matters

The current long-guide approach is strong as a reference manual, but it is too dense for first contact. A viewer in a discharge, rehab, long-term care, billing, or family-crisis moment needs orientation before detail.

The short guide should feel closer to a calm product experience than a government-style PDF. The goal is Robinhood-level simplicity with healthcare-worker credibility: clean cards, visual decision paths, plain-English warnings, and obvious next actions.

## Target reader

The short guide is for:

- adult children helping a parent,
- spouses and caregivers,
- patients overwhelmed by Medicare/Medicaid language,
- nurses/social workers/case managers who want a simple education handout,
- families dealing with discharge, rehab, nursing home care, Medicare Advantage, Medicaid, or confusing bills.

Assume the reader is stressed, impatient, and not looking for a textbook.

## Core product promise

**A 10-page visual guide that helps families understand what to ask before discharge, rehab, long-term care decisions, or paying a confusing Medicare-related bill.**

The short guide should not promise eligibility, coverage, legal advice, insurance advice, or bill adjudication. It should help readers identify the next right question and verify with official sources.

## Proposed 10-page structure

### Page 1 — Cover / positioning

Goal: make the guide instantly understandable and credible.

Content:

- Title: `Medicare, Medicaid, Rehab & Long-Term Care: A Family Quick Guide`
- Subtitle: `What to ask before discharge, rehab, nursing home care, or paying a confusing bill.`
- Byline: `Written from a healthcare-worker perspective by Andrew Ciccarelli, RN, BSN.`
- Small disclaimer: `Educational only. Verify rules with Medicare, Medicaid, your plan, provider, facility, SHIP, or a qualified professional.`
- QR to guide hub.

Visual direction:

- Full-page clean cover.
- One simple hero graphic: hospital -> home/rehab/long-term care/bill.
- No dense paragraph.

### Page 2 — The big picture: what problem are you solving?

Goal: orient the reader before they get lost in program names.

Content blocks:

- `Is this a medical coverage question?`
- `Is this a rehab/skilled care question?`
- `Is this a long-term daily support question?`
- `Is this a billing/document question?`

Visual direction:

- Four-card grid.
- Each card includes: problem, first question, where to verify.

### Page 3 — Medicare vs Medicaid in one page

Goal: clear the most common confusion.

Content:

- Medicare: federal health insurance; age/disability/conditions; hospital/doctor/drug/skilled care rules; can still leave costs.
- Medicaid: state-administered assistance under federal rules; eligibility varies; may matter for long-term services and supports.
- Dual eligibility: some people have both; exact category matters.

Visual direction:

- Split-screen comparison.
- Include a bottom warning: `Having both does not mean every service or bill is automatically covered.`

### Page 4 — The five mistakes families make

Goal: prevent the most expensive misunderstandings.

Content:

1. `Need care` does not automatically mean Medicare pays.
2. `Covered` does not always mean free.
3. `Rehab` is not the same as long-term custodial care.
4. Sleeping in a hospital bed does not always mean inpatient status.
5. A doctor order is not the same as plan approval.

Visual direction:

- Five warning cards.
- Use plain icons and short copy.
- No paragraphs over two lines.

### Page 5 — Discharge decision path

Goal: make discharge less chaotic.

Content flow:

- Confirm status: inpatient, outpatient, or observation.
- Confirm destination: home, home health, SNF/rehab, long-term care, other.
- Confirm payer: Original Medicare, Medicare Advantage, Medicaid, other.
- Confirm documents: notice, authorization, denial, appeal deadline, bill, EOB/MSN.
- Confirm backup plan if coverage is denied or ends.

Visual direction:

- Horizontal or vertical flowchart.
- Include a `Before you leave, ask this` box.

### Page 6 — Rehab and skilled nursing facility care

Goal: explain short-term skilled rehab without making promises.

Content:

- Rehab may be covered when rules are met.
- Original Medicare SNF coverage has specific rules, including skilled need and usually a qualifying inpatient stay unless an exception/waiver applies.
- Medicare Advantage may involve networks and prior authorization.
- Coverage can end if skilled need/documentation no longer supports it.

Visual direction:

- Timeline: hospital -> skilled rehab -> coverage review -> after skilled care.
- Include cost callout using verified 2026 numbers only if current verification remains active.

### Page 7 — Long-term care: the key warning

Goal: make the guide memorable and useful.

Content:

- Medicare generally does not pay for most long-term custodial care.
- Custodial care means help with bathing, dressing, toileting, eating, meals, mobility, supervision, and daily living.
- Medicaid may help if the person qualifies, but rules are state-specific.
- Ask about state Medicaid, LTSS, SHIP, aging resources, and legal guidance before major decisions.

Visual direction:

- Large central warning card.
- Skilled care vs custodial care comparison.
- Small `Do not DIY asset-transfer decisions` caution.

### Page 8 — Original Medicare vs Medicare Advantage

Goal: help readers know which process they are dealing with.

Content:

- Original Medicare: federal program, usually broader provider access if provider takes Medicare, no built-in annual out-of-pocket cap unless other coverage applies.
- Medicare Advantage: private Medicare-approved plan, may use networks, referrals, prior authorization, and yearly out-of-pocket limits for covered Medicare services.
- Neither option means every cost disappears.

Visual direction:

- Two-column comparison.
- Include `Ask before rehab/equipment/services: Is authorization required? Is the provider in network? What could we owe?`

### Page 9 — Before paying a confusing bill

Goal: stop people from paying blindly or panicking.

Content:

- A Medicare Summary Notice is not a bill.
- An Explanation of Benefits is not always a bill.
- One hospital visit can create multiple bills.
- Compare provider bill to MSN/EOB/plan paperwork before paying a confusing balance.

Visual direction:

- Three-document comparison: provider bill vs MSN/EOB vs next call.
- Include mini script:
  - `What date of service is this?`
  - `What did Medicare or the plan pay?`
  - `Why is this patient responsibility?`
  - `Is financial assistance available?`

### Page 10 — What to do next / QR directory

Goal: convert print distribution into site traffic and continued trust.

Content:

- `Use the full guide online.`
- `Estimate Medicare cost exposure.`
- `Compare a bill to an EOB/MSN.`
- `Read the long-term care article.`
- `Read the rehab-after-hospital article.`
- `Find SHIP or state Medicaid help.`
- Source/update note.
- Full disclaimer in condensed form.

Visual direction:

- QR directory with 4-6 codes maximum.
- Each QR has a plain-English caption.
- Use only production URLs, never preview URLs.

## What belongs in the long guide, not the short guide

Keep these in the long reference version:

- full 19-chapter explanation,
- full source notes and endnotes,
- detailed worksheets,
- expanded Medicaid dual-eligibility categories,
- estate recovery and spousal impoverishment details,
- extensive prior authorization explanation,
- exhaustive terminology sections,
- full methodology and update log.

The short guide can link to these. It should not try to carry them.

## Visual and layout rules for the short guide

- Aim for 10 pages, maximum 12 only if QR/source/disclaimer space forces it.
- Use visual hierarchy before prose.
- No paragraph should exceed 4 lines.
- No page should contain more than 3-5 major idea blocks.
- Use cards, flows, timelines, and comparison tables.
- Design for black-and-white printing first; color can enhance but must not be required.
- QR captions must describe the destination in plain English.
- Links should be printed as readable short paths under QR codes.
- Keep body text large enough for older adults and caregivers.
- Avoid fine print except source/update/footer language.
- Use consistent labels: `Ask`, `Verify`, `Watch out`, `Next step`.

## Trust and compliance guardrails

The short guide must not include:

- plan recommendations,
- insurer rankings,
- affiliate links,
- lead forms,
- sales language,
- individualized medical/legal/tax/insurance/Medicaid planning advice,
- Medicaid eligibility calculations,
- asset-transfer strategy,
- statements that a specific service or bill is definitely covered/not covered for a specific person.

Use cautious language:

- `may cover`,
- `when rules are met`,
- `verify with the plan`,
- `state rules vary`,
- `ask for the written notice`,
- `follow appeal instructions and deadlines`.

## Recommended implementation sequence

1. Keep the current comprehensive guide as the long reference product.
2. Create a new short-guide manuscript file, for example:
   - `docs/medicare-medicaid-quick-guide-visual-manuscript.md`
3. Create a dedicated short-guide PDF builder, for example:
   - `scripts/build-medicare-medicaid-quick-guide-pdf.mjs`
4. Create a new manual artifact workflow, for example:
   - `.github/workflows/medicare-medicaid-quick-guide-pdf.yml`
5. Generate a draft artifact only; do not place it under `public/guides/` until QA passes.
6. Review draft artifact on desktop, iPhone, and black-and-white print.
7. Add final short-guide PDF under:
   - `/public/guides/medicare-medicaid-family-quick-guide.pdf`
8. Add final long-guide PDF later under:
   - `/public/guides/hospital-family-guide-medicare-medicaid-rehab-long-term-care.pdf`
9. Update the landing page so the short guide is the primary download and the long guide is optional.
10. Add sitemap URLs only after the public files exist and return 200.

`Use the Medicare cost estimator`

## Product acceptance criteria

The short guide is ready when a new viewer can answer these questions in under two minutes:

1. What is the difference between Medicare and Medicaid?
2. Why might Medicare not pay for long-term custodial care?
3. What should I ask before discharge or rehab?
4. What should I compare before paying a confusing bill?
5. Where do I go online for the full guide/tools?

If the short guide cannot do those five things quickly, it is still too dense.
