# Chapter 14 — Skilled Care vs Custodial Care

## Direct answer

Skilled care requires licensed nursing or therapy skills. Custodial care is ongoing help with daily living, such as bathing, dressing, toileting, eating, mobility, meals, supervision, and transportation. Medicare treats these differently.

## Plain-English explanation

This is one of the most important distinctions in the entire guide. A person can truly need custodial help and still not have Medicare coverage for most long-term daily support. That does not mean the need is fake. It means the need may belong in a different payment category: Medicaid/LTSS for eligible people, private pay, family support, community programs, or other resources.

## Common misunderstanding

Families may think the word "care" means one thing. Medicare separates types of care very specifically.

## Hospital/caregiver example

A patient needs wound care and physical therapy after hospitalization. That may be skilled. Later, the wound is healed, therapy ends, and the person mostly needs help bathing and getting meals. That ongoing help may be custodial.

## Questions to ask

- Which needs are skilled?
- Which needs are daily living or custodial?
- Who documented the skilled need?
- What payer is being asked to cover each need?
- If Medicare will not cover it, should Medicaid/LTSS or community supports be explored?
