# Chapter 15 — Long-Term Care and Medicaid

## Direct answer

Medicare generally does not pay for most long-term custodial care. Medicaid is the primary payer across the country for long-term care services, but eligibility and services depend on state rules.

## Plain-English explanation

Long-term care may happen in a nursing home, assisted living setting, home, adult day program, or community-based setting. It often involves ongoing support for chronic illness, disability, frailty, or daily living needs.

Medicare may cover limited skilled care, but it does not solve most long-term custodial care costs. Medicaid may help people who qualify. Because Medicaid is state-administered, families must verify state-specific rules.

## Common misunderstanding

Families often wait until the patient needs placement before asking about Medicaid. By then, the situation may be urgent and paperwork-heavy.

## Hospital/caregiver example

A person with dementia cannot safely live alone and needs supervision, meals, and help bathing. Medicare may not cover that ongoing daily support. The family may need to contact the state Medicaid agency, SHIP, an elder-law attorney, or local aging resources.

## Questions to ask

- Is the need long-term custodial care?
- What state Medicaid agency applies?
- Are there home- and community-based services?
- What documents are needed?
- Is professional legal or benefits guidance appropriate?
- Is there a local long-term care ombudsman or aging-services agency?
