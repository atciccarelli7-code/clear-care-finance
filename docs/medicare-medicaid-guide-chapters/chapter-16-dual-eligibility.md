# Chapter 16 — Dual Eligibility

## Direct answer

Dual eligibility means a person has both Medicare and Medicaid. Medicare usually pays first for Medicare-covered services, and Medicaid may help with certain costs or services depending on state rules and the person's eligibility category.

## Plain-English explanation

Having both Medicare and Medicaid can reduce costs, but it can also create coordination questions. The person may have Medicare, Medicaid, a Medicare Advantage plan, a Part D plan, or a coordinated dual-eligible plan. The exact setup matters.

A dual-eligible patient may still need to verify provider participation, plan rules, state Medicaid rules, authorization requirements, and which card applies to which service.

## Common misunderstanding

Families sometimes think having both programs means everything is covered without paperwork. In reality, provider participation, plan rules, state Medicaid rules, authorizations, and documentation can still matter.

## Hospital/caregiver example

A patient has Medicare and Medicaid. Medicare handles many medical services first. Medicaid may help with cost-sharing or long-term support, depending on the state and eligibility category. The family still needs to confirm provider acceptance and plan rules before assuming a service is covered.

## Questions to ask

- Does the patient have full Medicaid or limited Medicaid assistance?
- Which card should be used for this service?
- Does the provider accept both Medicare and Medicaid?
- Is there a coordinated plan?
- Who can explain the benefits: Medicaid agency, SHIP, the plan, or provider?
