# Page 1 — Before Discharge: Questions Families Should Ask

## A plain-English checklist for Medicare, Medicaid, rehab, home health, and bills

Hospital discharge can move quickly. This handout is designed to help families slow down, organize the next questions, and understand who can verify the answer.

This is not a substitute for the care team, Medicare, Medicaid, the plan, the facility, SHIP, or a qualified professional.

## 1. What is the patient’s hospital status?

Ask:

- Is the patient inpatient, outpatient, or observation?
- When did inpatient admission begin, if it began?
- Does this status affect skilled nursing facility or rehab coverage?
- Is there a written notice we should review?

Why it matters:

A patient can stay overnight in a hospital without every hour counting the same way for Medicare coverage rules.

## 2. What setting is being recommended after discharge?

Ask:

- Home without services?
- Home health?
- Outpatient therapy?
- Skilled nursing facility rehab?
- Inpatient rehab?
- Long-term care or custodial support?

Why it matters:

Different settings can trigger different payer rules, facility requirements, authorizations, and patient costs.

## 3. What skilled need is documented?

Ask:

- What skilled nursing or therapy need is being ordered?
- Who documented it?
- How often will the service be provided?
- What would cause coverage to stop?

Why it matters:

Medicare-covered rehab and skilled nursing facility care usually depend on skilled needs, not only on whether the patient still needs help.

## 4. Who is expected to pay?

Ask:

- Original Medicare?
- Medicare Advantage?
- Medicaid?
- Medigap?
- Retiree or employer coverage?
- Private pay?

Why it matters:

The payer determines which rules matter: network, prior authorization, cost-sharing, certification, eligibility, or state Medicaid rules.

## 5. Has authorization been approved?

Ask:

- Is prior authorization required?
- Who submitted it?
- When was it submitted?
- Is it approved, denied, pending, or partially approved?
- What is the reference number?
- What is the appeal deadline if denied?

Why it matters:

A clinician recommendation and plan approval are not the same thing.

# Page 2 — Rehab, Long-Term Care, and Bills: What to Verify

## 6. Is this skilled care or custodial care?

Ask:

- Which needs are skilled?
- Which needs are daily living or custodial needs?
- Is the patient receiving nursing or therapy care that requires professional skill?
- Is the main need help with bathing, dressing, meals, toileting, supervision, or transportation?

Why it matters:

Medicare treats skilled care and long-term custodial care differently. A patient can truly need help and still not have Medicare coverage for most long-term daily support.

## 7. If long-term care is the issue, should Medicaid be explored?

Ask:

- Which state Medicaid agency applies?
- Are there long-term services and supports in this state?
- What documents are needed?
- Should the family contact SHIP, the state Medicaid agency, an elder-law attorney, or local aging resources?

Why it matters:

Medicare generally does not pay for most long-term custodial care. Medicaid may become relevant for people who meet state rules.

## 8. What could the patient owe?

Ask:

- Is there a deductible?
- Is there a copay or coinsurance?
- Is there a daily skilled nursing facility amount?
- Is the provider or facility in-network?
- What is not covered?
- Can we get the estimate in writing?

Why it matters:

Covered does not always mean free. Medicare or a plan may pay part of a claim while the patient still owes cost-sharing.

## 9. Before paying a bill, what should be matched?

Ask:

- What date of service is this?
- Which provider or facility is billing?
- Has Medicare or the plan processed the claim?
- Does the bill match the Medicare Summary Notice or Explanation of Benefits?
- What was the allowed amount?
- What did the plan pay?
- Why is this listed as patient responsibility?
- Is financial assistance available?

Why it matters:

One episode of care can create multiple bills. Compare the bill to the plan paperwork before paying a confusing balance.
