# 1. Who this guide is for

## Direct answer

This guide is for patients, caregivers, adult children, spouses, and families trying to understand Medicare, Medicaid, rehab, home health, skilled nursing facility care, long-term care, and confusing medical bills.

## Plain-English explanation

Families often meet Medicare and Medicaid rules at the worst possible time: someone is hospitalized, weak, unsafe at home, being discharged, choosing rehab, entering a facility, or receiving a bill. The system uses precise words like inpatient, observation, skilled care, custodial care, coinsurance, prior authorization, and allowed amount. Those words matter because they can change what gets paid.

This guide is meant to slow the situation down. It will not decide your case. It will help you understand what question to ask next.

## Common misunderstanding

The biggest misunderstanding is thinking, "The patient needs care, so Medicare must pay for it." Need and coverage are related, but they are not the same.

## Example

A parent leaves the hospital after a fall. The family hears "rehab" and assumes Medicare will cover the stay. The better first question is: what skilled care is being ordered, what hospital status applies, what facility is being used, and what payer rules must be met?

## Questions to ask

- What problem are we trying to solve right now: hospital bill, discharge plan, rehab, home health, long-term care, or Medicaid eligibility?
- Which payer is involved: Original Medicare, Medicare Advantage, Medicaid, employer retiree coverage, Medigap, or another plan?
- Who can verify the answer in writing?

# 2. Disclaimer and boundaries

## Direct answer

This guide teaches concepts and questions. It does not give personal advice or determine coverage, eligibility, medical necessity, discharge safety, or legal strategy.

## Plain-English explanation

Medicare and Medicaid decisions can depend on details: state, plan, facility, diagnosis, timing, documentation, provider status, prior authorization, income, resources, and the exact service being requested. A plain-English guide can help you ask better questions, but it cannot replace official verification.

## Common misunderstanding

A family may read a general rule and assume it applies exactly to their situation. That can be dangerous. General education should lead to better verification, not replace it.

## Example

A guide can explain that Medicare generally does not pay for most long-term custodial care. But whether a specific person qualifies for Medicaid long-term services depends on state rules and personal facts.

## Questions to ask

- Which official source applies to this situation?
- Is this Original Medicare, Medicare Advantage, Medicaid, or another payer?
- Is there a written notice, denial, authorization, plan document, bill, EOB, or Medicare Summary Notice?
- Should we contact SHIP, the state Medicaid agency, the plan, the facility, the billing office, or a qualified professional?

# 3. The 5 biggest misunderstandings

## Direct answer

The five biggest misunderstandings are: Medicare and Medicaid are different; covered does not always mean free; skilled care is different from custodial care; sleeping in a hospital bed does not automatically mean inpatient status; and Medicare Advantage can involve networks and prior authorization.

## Plain-English explanation

These five points explain most of the confusion families face after hospitalization. A person can be very sick and still run into coverage rules. A service can be covered and still leave a bill. A rehab stay can be medically recommended but still require documentation, facility rules, and authorization. Long-term daily help can be necessary but still outside what Medicare usually pays for.

## Common misunderstanding

Families often think one card solves everything. In reality, Medicare, Medicaid, Medigap, Medicare Advantage, Part D, hospital billing, facility billing, and state rules may all touch different parts of the same situation.

## Example

A patient leaves the hospital, enters rehab, improves slowly, and still cannot bathe or dress alone. Medicare may have covered part of the skilled rehab stay. That does not mean Medicare will pay indefinitely for daily custodial help.

## Questions to ask

- Is this Medicare or Medicaid?
- Is this skilled care or custodial care?
- Is the patient inpatient or observation?
- Is the provider or facility in-network or Medicare-certified?
- Is prior authorization required?
- What could the patient owe?

# 4. Medicare vs Medicaid

## Direct answer

Medicare is federal health insurance mainly tied to age, disability, or certain conditions. Medicaid is a state-administered assistance program under federal rules that can help eligible people with healthcare and, in many cases, long-term services and supports.

## Plain-English explanation

Medicare and Medicaid sound similar, but they answer different questions. Medicare asks: are you eligible for federal health insurance through age, disability, or qualifying condition? Medicaid asks: do you meet your state's eligibility rules for assistance based on income, resources, disability category, family status, age, medical need, or other state-specific criteria?

Some people have both. They are often called dual eligible. For Medicare-covered services, Medicare usually pays first. Medicaid may help with premiums, cost-sharing, or services Medicare does not cover, depending on the person's state and eligibility category.

## Common misunderstanding

Many families believe Medicare will pay for nursing home care because the patient is older. Medicare is health insurance. Medicaid is often the program that becomes relevant when long-term services and supports are the issue.

## Example

A 79-year-old patient has Medicare and needs short-term skilled therapy after a hospital stay. Medicare may be the first coverage question. Months later, the patient mainly needs help with bathing, dressing, meals, and supervision. Medicaid eligibility may become the larger question.

## Questions to ask

- Does the patient have Medicare, Medicaid, both, or neither?
- Is the service medical care, skilled care, daily living help, or long-term support?
- Which state Medicaid agency should be contacted?
- Is SHIP available to help explain Medicare options?

# 5. Medicare Parts A, B, C, D, and Medigap

## Direct answer

Medicare has parts. Part A is commonly associated with inpatient hospital and certain post-hospital care. Part B covers many outpatient and physician services. Part C is Medicare Advantage. Part D is drug coverage. Medigap is supplemental coverage used with Original Medicare, not Medicare Advantage.

## Plain-English explanation

A Medicare card does not tell the whole story. People first need to know how they receive coverage. Original Medicare includes Part A and Part B. A person with Original Medicare may also have Part D and Medigap. Medicare Advantage is another way to receive Medicare benefits through a private plan that must follow Medicare rules but may have networks, prior authorization, and plan-specific costs.

## Common misunderstanding

People often ask, "Does Medicare cover this?" The better question is, "Which part, plan, provider, facility, pharmacy, and rule applies?"

## Example

A person has Original Medicare, a Part D drug plan, and Medigap. Another person has Medicare Advantage with drug coverage included. They both have Medicare, but their provider access, bills, authorizations, and drug costs may work differently.

## Questions to ask

- Does the patient have Original Medicare or Medicare Advantage?
- Does the patient have Part D drug coverage?
- Does the patient have Medigap, Medicaid, employer retiree coverage, or another supplement?
- Which card should be shown for this service?

# 6. Original Medicare vs Medicare Advantage

## Direct answer

Original Medicare and Medicare Advantage are two different ways to receive Medicare coverage. Original Medicare generally offers broader provider access among Medicare-accepting providers, while Medicare Advantage often uses plan networks, plan cost-sharing, and prior authorization rules.

## Plain-English explanation

Original Medicare is run through the federal Medicare program. Medicare Advantage is offered by private plans approved by Medicare. Medicare Advantage plans must cover almost all medically necessary services Original Medicare covers, but they can operate with plan rules such as networks, referrals, and prior authorization.

Original Medicare usually does not have a built-in annual out-of-pocket maximum unless the person has supplemental coverage. Medicare Advantage plans have yearly limits for covered Medicare services, but costs, networks, drugs, and authorization rules vary by plan.

## Common misunderstanding

A low or zero premium does not mean a plan has zero medical cost risk. Premium is only one part of the total cost.

## Example

A patient with Original Medicare may be able to use any hospital or doctor that accepts Medicare. A patient with Medicare Advantage may need an in-network facility for non-emergency care and may need authorization before rehab or certain services.

## Questions to ask

- Is this Original Medicare or Medicare Advantage?
- Is the provider or facility in-network?
- Is prior authorization required?
- What is the maximum out-of-pocket limit for covered Medicare services?
- Are drugs included, or is there a separate Part D plan?

# 7. What Medicare does not cover

## Direct answer

Medicare covers many important services, but it does not cover everything. One of the biggest gaps is most long-term custodial care: ongoing help with daily activities like bathing, dressing, toileting, meals, transportation, and supervision.

## Plain-English explanation

A service can be important and still not be covered. Medicare is primarily health insurance, not a full long-term care funding system. Original Medicare also has common limits around routine dental, vision, hearing, foreign travel, and non-medical home support. Some Medicare Advantage plans may offer extra benefits, but those vary by plan and do not erase the need to verify details.

## Common misunderstanding

Families often think that if a doctor says a patient is unsafe at home, Medicare must cover daily help or long-term placement. Unsafe at home is serious, but it does not automatically create Medicare coverage for custodial care.

## Example

A patient finishes covered skilled rehab but still needs help bathing and dressing every day. That need is real. The family still has to ask whether the next need is skilled care, custodial care, Medicaid/LTSS, private pay, family caregiving, or community support.

## Questions to ask

- Is this service skilled care or custodial care?
- Is there a Medicare benefit category for this service?
- Is the service covered under Original Medicare or the Medicare Advantage plan?
- What would the patient owe if it is not covered?
- Should Medicaid or a state program be explored?

# 8. Why Medicare can pay and the patient can still owe

## Direct answer

Medicare payment does not always mean the patient's bill is zero. Premiums, deductibles, copays, coinsurance, drug costs, non-covered services, provider status, and plan rules can still create patient responsibility.

## Plain-English explanation

Insurance payment and full payment are not the same. Medicare or a plan may approve a service and pay its share. The patient may still owe a deductible, 20% coinsurance, a fixed copay, a daily facility amount, or the cost of services that were not covered. Separate providers can also bill separately for one episode of care.

## Common misunderstanding

Patients sometimes assume a bill is wrong simply because Medicare already paid something. The bill may be wrong, but it may also reflect normal cost-sharing. The answer is to compare the provider bill against the Medicare Summary Notice or Explanation of Benefits before paying.

## Example

A patient gets an outpatient service covered under Part B. Medicare approves the service and pays its share. The patient still owes coinsurance after the deductible because covered does not always mean free.

## Questions to ask

- Is this a premium, deductible, copay, coinsurance, non-covered charge, or out-of-network issue?
- Does the bill match the Medicare Summary Notice or EOB?
- What is the allowed amount?
- Did the provider accept assignment or participate in the plan network?
- Is financial assistance available?

# 9. Inpatient vs observation

## Direct answer

A patient can stay overnight in a hospital and still not be formally admitted as inpatient. Observation time may not count the same way for certain Medicare coverage rules, including some skilled nursing facility coverage rules.

## Plain-English explanation

Hospital status is not just about where the patient sleeps. It is a billing and coverage classification. For some Medicare rules, especially skilled nursing facility coverage under Original Medicare, inpatient status and timing can matter. Families should ask the hospital directly: is the patient inpatient, outpatient, or observation?

## Common misunderstanding

Families often think, "They stayed three nights, so rehab is covered." The more precise question is whether those nights counted as qualifying inpatient days under the applicable rule.

## Example

A patient spends one night in the emergency department, one night under observation, and then is admitted inpatient. The family should not assume all nights count the same way toward SNF coverage.

## Questions to ask

- What is the patient's official hospital status right now?
- When did inpatient admission begin, if it began?
- Does observation time count for the next service being requested?
- Has the hospital provided any required notice?
- How does the Medicare Advantage plan handle this, if applicable?

# 10. What to ask before discharge

## Direct answer

Before discharge, families should ask where the patient is going, what care is needed, who is expected to pay, what authorization is required, what the patient may owe, and what the backup plan is if coverage is denied or ends.

## Plain-English explanation

Discharge can move quickly. Families may hear terms like rehab, home health, walker, oxygen, therapy, skilled nursing, or facility placement without understanding the coverage path. Asking early is better than trying to fix confusion after the patient is already moved.

## Common misunderstanding

Families may think discharge planning is only about where the patient goes. It is also about payer rules, documentation, equipment, medications, transportation, follow-up appointments, and safety.

## Example

A patient is recommended for skilled rehab. The family asks whether the facility is Medicare-certified or in-network, whether authorization is approved, what the daily cost could be, and what happens if coverage stops before the patient is independent.

## Questions to ask

- What is the recommended discharge setting?
- What skilled need is documented?
- What services are ordered?
- Which payer is expected to pay?
- Is authorization approved?
- What could the patient owe?
- What happens if coverage is denied or ends?

# 11. Rehab after a hospital stay

## Direct answer

Medicare may cover short-term skilled rehab after a hospital stay when rules are met, but coverage is not automatic. The patient usually needs skilled care, the facility must meet requirements, and plan rules may apply.

## Plain-English explanation

Rehab is often where families first discover the difference between needing help and meeting coverage rules. Original Medicare has specific requirements for skilled nursing facility coverage. Medicare Advantage plans can apply network and prior authorization rules and may operate differently from Original Medicare.

## Common misunderstanding

A doctor recommending rehab does not guarantee payment. The payer still applies coverage rules.

## Example

A patient leaves the hospital weak after pneumonia. Therapy recommends SNF rehab. The family should ask about hospital status, skilled need, facility certification, authorization, network, daily cost, and coverage-ending criteria.

## Questions to ask

- What skilled service is being ordered?
- Is the facility Medicare-certified or in-network?
- Has authorization been approved?
- What days or cost-sharing might apply?
- What happens if the patient still needs help after rehab ends?

# 12. Skilled nursing facility care

## Direct answer

Skilled nursing facility care is short-term care that may be covered by Medicare Part A for eligible patients when specific rules are met. It is not the same as long-term custodial nursing home care.

## Plain-English explanation

A skilled nursing facility can provide nursing care, therapy, medications, medical social services, meals, and other covered services during a qualifying stay. The key word is skilled. The care must require professional nursing or therapy skills and meet coverage rules.

Under Original Medicare, SNF coverage is limited within a benefit period and can involve daily coinsurance after the early covered days. Medicare Advantage plans may apply different operational rules, including authorization and network requirements.

## Common misunderstanding

Families hear "nursing facility" and assume all nursing home care is Medicare-covered. Medicare-covered SNF care is limited and short-term; long-term custodial care is different.

## Example

A patient needs daily physical therapy and skilled nursing after surgery. A SNF stay may be covered if requirements are met. Later, if the patient only needs help with meals, bathing, dressing, and supervision, the coverage question changes.

## Questions to ask

- Is the patient receiving daily skilled care?
- Is the facility Medicare-certified or in-network?
- How many days are expected to be covered?
- What is the cost for days 21-100, if applicable?
- What signs could cause coverage to end?

# 13. Home health and durable medical equipment

## Direct answer

Medicare may cover certain home health services and durable medical equipment when rules are met, but it does not pay for 24-hour home care, meal delivery, homemaker services unrelated to the care plan, or custodial care when that is the only care needed.

## Plain-English explanation

Home health is not the same as having a full-time aide at home. Medicare-covered home health generally involves skilled nursing or therapy needs, a provider order, homebound status, and a Medicare-certified home health agency. DME coverage also depends on medical necessity, provider orders, supplier enrollment, and assignment rules.

## Common misunderstanding

Families often hear "home health" and expect daily long-term personal care. Medicare home health can be helpful, but it is not a substitute for full-time custodial support.

## Example

A patient is discharged with a walker and home physical therapy. Medicare may cover certain services and DME when requirements are met. If the family also wants someone to cook meals and stay all day for supervision, that is a different coverage question.

## Questions to ask

- What home health services are ordered?
- Is the agency Medicare-certified or plan-approved?
- Does the patient meet the homebound requirement?
- Is the equipment supplier enrolled and accepting assignment?
- What could the patient owe for DME?

# 14. Skilled care vs custodial care

## Direct answer

Skilled care requires licensed nursing or therapy skills. Custodial care is ongoing help with daily living, such as bathing, dressing, toileting, eating, mobility, meals, supervision, and transportation. Medicare treats these differently.

## Plain-English explanation

This is one of the most important distinctions in the entire guide. A person can truly need custodial help and still not have Medicare coverage for most long-term daily support. That does not mean the need is fake. It means the need may belong in a different payment category: Medicaid/LTSS for eligible people, private pay, family support, community programs, or other resources.

## Common misunderstanding

Families may think the word "care" means one thing. Medicare separates types of care very specifically.

## Example

A patient needs wound care and physical therapy after hospitalization. That may be skilled. Later, the wound is healed, therapy ends, and the person mostly needs help bathing and getting meals. That ongoing help may be custodial.

## Questions to ask

- Which needs are skilled?
- Which needs are daily living or custodial?
- Who documented the skilled need?
- What payer is being asked to cover each need?
- If Medicare will not cover it, should Medicaid/LTSS or community supports be explored?

# 15. Long-term care and Medicaid

## Direct answer

Medicare generally does not pay for most long-term custodial care. Medicaid is the primary payer across the country for long-term care services, but eligibility and services depend on state rules.

## Plain-English explanation

Long-term care may happen in a nursing home, assisted living setting, home, adult day program, or community-based setting. It often involves ongoing support for chronic illness, disability, frailty, or daily living needs. Medicare may cover limited skilled care, but it does not solve most long-term custodial care costs.

Medicaid may help people who qualify. Because Medicaid is state-administered, families must verify state-specific rules.

## Common misunderstanding

Families wait until the patient needs placement before asking about Medicaid. By then, the situation may be urgent and paperwork-heavy.

## Example

A person with dementia cannot safely live alone and needs supervision, meals, and help bathing. Medicare may not cover that ongoing daily support. The family may need to contact the state Medicaid agency, SHIP, an elder-law attorney, or local aging resources.

## Questions to ask

- Is the need long-term custodial care?
- What state Medicaid agency applies?
- Are there home- and community-based services?
- What documents are needed?
- Is professional legal or benefits guidance appropriate?

# 16. Dual eligibility

## Direct answer

Dual eligibility means a person has both Medicare and Medicaid. Medicare usually pays first for Medicare-covered services, and Medicaid may help with certain costs or services depending on state rules and eligibility category.

## Plain-English explanation

Having both programs can reduce costs, but it can also create coordination questions. The person may have Medicare, Medicaid, a Medicare Advantage plan, a Part D plan, or a special type of coordinated plan. The exact setup matters.

## Common misunderstanding

Families sometimes think having both programs means everything is covered without paperwork. In reality, provider participation, plan rules, state Medicaid rules, authorizations, and documentation can still matter.

## Example

A patient has Medicare and Medicaid. Medicare handles many medical services first. Medicaid may help with cost-sharing or long-term support, depending on the state and eligibility. The family still needs to confirm provider acceptance and plan rules.

## Questions to ask

- Does the patient have full Medicaid or limited Medicaid assistance?
- Which card should be used for this service?
- Does the provider accept both Medicare and Medicaid?
- Is there a coordinated plan?
- Who can explain the benefits: Medicaid agency, SHIP, plan, or provider?

# 17. Medicare Advantage prior authorization

## Direct answer

Prior authorization means a plan may require approval before it agrees to cover a service, facility stay, equipment, drug, or treatment. Medicare Advantage plans may require prior authorization for some services and supplies.

## Plain-English explanation

Prior authorization is not the same as a doctor's recommendation. A clinician may recommend care, but the plan may still require documentation and approval before payment. Delays, denials, and appeals can happen. Families should document dates, reference numbers, who submitted the request, and deadlines.

## Common misunderstanding

Families often think, "The doctor ordered it, so the plan approved it." Those are separate steps.

## Example

A patient needs rehab after hospitalization. The facility and care team recommend placement, but the Medicare Advantage plan must authorize the stay. The family should ask whether authorization has been submitted, approved, denied, or is still pending.

## Questions to ask

- Is prior authorization required?
- Who submitted it?
- When was it submitted?
- What is the reference number?
- Is it approved, denied, pending, or partially approved?
- What is the appeal deadline if denied?

# 18. Bills, EOBs, Medicare Summary Notices, and scripts

## Direct answer

Before paying a confusing bill, compare the bill to the Medicare Summary Notice or Explanation of Benefits. Confirm the date of service, provider, allowed amount, plan payment, and patient responsibility.

## Plain-English explanation

One hospital visit can create multiple bills. A facility may bill separately from physicians, radiology, lab, ambulance, anesthesia, therapy, or outside professionals. The first bill is not always the full story, and it is not always correct.

The goal is not to avoid legitimate bills. The goal is to understand what the bill represents before paying.

## Common misunderstanding

Patients may believe one hospital bill includes everything. Many medical bills come from separate legal entities or provider groups.

## Example

A patient receives a hospital facility bill and a separate physician bill for the same date. The family checks both against the EOB or Medicare Summary Notice and asks why each balance is listed as patient responsibility.

## Questions to ask the billing office

- What date of service is this?
- Which provider or facility is billing me?
- Has Medicare or the plan processed this claim?
- What was the allowed amount?
- What did the plan pay?
- Why is this my responsibility?
- Is financial assistance available?

# Closing note

You do not have to understand every Medicare or Medicaid rule to ask better questions. Start with the situation in front of you: discharge, rehab, home health, long-term care, Medicaid, a denial, or a bill. Then ask which payer is involved, what rule applies, what documentation exists, what the patient may owe, and where the answer can be verified.
