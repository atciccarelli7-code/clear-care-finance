## 1. Medicare.gov — How Medicare works

Source URL: https://www.medicare.gov/basics/get-started-with-medicare/medicare-basics/how-does-medicare-work

### Usable facts

- People generally sign up for Part A and Part B, then choose how they receive Medicare health coverage.
- A person can choose Original Medicare or Medicare Advantage for health coverage.
- Original Medicare includes Part A and Part B.
- Original Medicare covers many approved services and supplies, but not all costs.
- Original Medicare does not have a built-in yearly out-of-pocket limit unless the person has other coverage such as Medigap, Medicaid, employer, retiree, or union coverage.
- Medicare Advantage bundles Part A, Part B, and usually Part D into one plan.
- Medicare Advantage plans may have rules for how services are received, including referrals and network requirements.
- Medicare Advantage plans must cover all emergency and urgent care and almost all medically necessary services Original Medicare covers.

### Best guide use

Use in the basic map chapter explaining Part A, Part B, Original Medicare, Medicare Advantage, Part D, Medigap, and why coverage choice affects bills and provider access.

## 2. Medicare.gov — Compare Original Medicare and Medicare Advantage

Source URL: https://www.medicare.gov/basics/get-started-with-medicare/get-more-coverage/your-coverage-options/compare-original-medicare-medicare-advantage

### Usable facts

- Original Medicare generally lets beneficiaries use any doctor or hospital that takes Medicare in the United States.
- Medicare Advantage may require the patient to use network providers for non-emergency care.
- Original Medicare usually charges 20% coinsurance for Part B-covered services after the Part B deductible.
- Medicare Advantage costs vary by plan.
- Original Medicare has no yearly out-of-pocket limit unless the person has supplemental coverage.
- Medicare Advantage plans have yearly limits for covered Medicare services.
- Medigap can be used with Original Medicare, but not to cover Medicare Advantage out-of-pocket costs.
- Original Medicare usually does not require prior authorization for covered services and supplies.
- Medicare Advantage plans may require prior authorization for some services or supplies.

### Best guide use

Use in chapters comparing Original Medicare and Medicare Advantage, explaining prior authorization, network risk, and why a low premium is not the same as low total cost.

## 3. Medicare.gov — Medicare costs

Source URL: https://www.medicare.gov/basics/costs/medicare-costs

### Usable facts

- What a person pays for Medicare varies based on coverage, services, and providers.
- Original Medicare has no yearly out-of-pocket limit unless the person has supplemental coverage or joins Medicare Advantage.
- 2026 Part A inpatient hospital deductible: $1,736 per benefit period.
- 2026 Part A inpatient hospital coinsurance: days 1-60 are $0 after the deductible; days 61-90 are $434 per day; lifetime reserve days are $868 per day; after day 150, the patient pays all costs.
- 2026 skilled nursing facility costs: days 1-20 are $0; days 21-100 are $217 per day; days 101 and beyond are all costs.
- Covered home health care services are $0, while covered durable medical equipment generally has 20% coinsurance after the Part B deductible.
- 2026 Part B premium: $202.90 per month for most people, with possible higher income-related amounts.
- 2026 Part B deductible: $283.
- Part B services usually involve 20% coinsurance after the deductible when the provider accepts assignment.
- Medicare Advantage costs vary by plan and the person must keep paying the Part B premium.
- Part D premiums, deductibles, copays, and coinsurance vary by plan and pharmacy.
- Medigap premiums vary by policy, location, and other factors.

### Best guide use

Use in cost examples, bill explanations, rehab chapters, and the Medicare-cost exposure worksheet. Recheck all dollar amounts before final publication.

## 4. Medicare.gov — Long-term care

Source URL: https://www.medicare.gov/coverage/long-term-care

### Usable facts

- Medicare does not pay for long-term care.
- Long-term care is also called custodial care or long-term services and supports.
- Long-term care can include medical and non-medical care for people with chronic illness or disability.
- Most long-term care helps with activities of daily living, such as dressing, bathing, bathroom use, meals, adult day health care, and transportation.
- Long-term care is different from skilled nursing facility care.
- Most health insurance, including Medigap, does not pay for long-term care services.
- People may be eligible for long-term care through Medicaid if they meet state eligibility rules.
- People pay all costs for non-covered services, including most long-term care.

### Best guide use

This is the anchor source for the long-term care chapter and the skilled-care-vs-custodial-care worksheet. It should appear early in the guide because it is one of the biggest family misunderstandings.

## 5. Medicare.gov — Skilled nursing facility care

Source URL: https://www.medicare.gov/coverage/skilled-nursing-facility-care

### Usable facts

- Medicare Part A covers skilled nursing facility care for eligible beneficiaries for a limited time on a short-term basis.
- Skilled care means nursing or therapy care that can only be safely and effectively performed by, or under the supervision of, professionals or technical personnel.
- Medicare-covered SNF services can include semi-private room, meals, skilled nursing care, therapy, medical social services, medications, supplies, equipment used in the facility, and certain ambulance transportation.
- Medicare generally requires a qualifying inpatient hospital stay of at least 3 days in a row, not counting the discharge day.
- Time spent under observation or in the emergency room before inpatient admission does not count toward the 3-day qualifying inpatient hospital stay, even if the patient was there overnight.
- The patient generally must enter the SNF within a short time after leaving the hospital.
- The patient must need daily skilled care and receive it in a Medicare-certified SNF.
- 2026 SNF costs: days 1-20 are $0 after the Part A deductible context; days 21-100 are $217 per day; days 101 and beyond are all costs.
- Part A limits SNF coverage to 100 days in each benefit period.
- Medicare Advantage plans may waive the 3-day minimum inpatient hospital stay, but the patient should contact the plan for rules.

### Best guide use

Use in rehab-after-hospital, SNF, observation-status, and discharge checklist chapters. This source should drive the questions families ask before choosing rehab.

## 6. Medicare.gov — Home health services

Source URL: https://www.medicare.gov/coverage/home-health-services

### Usable facts

- Medicare covers certain home health services under Part A and Part B if eligibility rules are met.
- Home health can include skilled nursing, therapy, medical social services, part-time or intermittent home health aide care when tied to skilled services, DME, and medical supplies for home use.
- Medicare does not pay for 24-hour-a-day care at home, home meal delivery, homemaker services unrelated to the care plan, or custodial/personal care when that is the only care needed.
- The patient must need part-time or intermittent skilled services and meet the homebound definition.
- A provider must order the care, and a Medicare-certified home health agency must provide it.
- Covered home health services are $0, but DME usually involves 20% coinsurance after the Part B deductible.
- The home health agency should tell the patient if Medicare will not pay for certain items or services and should give an Advance Beneficiary Notice when required.

### Best guide use

Use to explain that home health is not full-time home aide care and is not the same as long-term custodial care.

## 7. Medicare.gov — Durable medical equipment

Source URL: https://www.medicare.gov/coverage/durable-medical-equipment-dme-coverage

### Usable facts

- Medicare Part B covers medically necessary durable medical equipment when eligibility rules are met.
- DME must be durable, used for a medical reason, typically useful only to someone sick or injured, used in the home, and expected to last at least 3 years.
- Examples include canes, commode chairs, CPAP equipment, crutches, glucose monitors and supplies, hospital beds, oxygen equipment, walkers, wheelchairs, and scooters.
- DME may need to be rented, purchased, or rented until ownership transfers depending on the item.
- A doctor or other provider must order DME for use in the home.
- Doctors and suppliers should be enrolled in Medicare.
- Patients should ask whether the supplier participates in Medicare and accepts assignment.
- After the Part B deductible, the patient generally pays 20% of the Medicare-approved amount if the supplier accepts assignment.
- If a supplier does not participate or does not accept assignment, the patient may owe more.

### Best guide use

Use in the home health/DME chapter and the discharge checklist. This source supports practical questions before ordering equipment.

## 8. Medicaid.gov — Long-term services and supports

Source URL: https://www.medicaid.gov/medicaid/long-term-services-supports

### Usable facts

- Medicaid is the primary payer across the country for long-term care services.
- Medicaid LTSS can cover services across institutional care and community-based settings.
- LTSS serves people with disabling conditions and chronic illnesses, including children, adults, and seniors.
- Medicaid LTSS policy emphasizes person-driven, inclusive, effective, sustainable, coordinated, and culturally competent support systems.
- Medicaid LTSS is tied to state-administered programs and rules.

### Best guide use

Use in the chapter explaining when Medicaid becomes the real question for long-term care. This is the main official source for why Medicaid matters when Medicare does not cover ongoing custodial needs.

## 9. Medicaid.gov — Eligibility policy

Source URL: https://www.medicaid.gov/medicaid/eligibility-policy

### Usable facts

- Medicaid eligibility is governed by federal rules and state administration.
- Medicaid rules vary across eligibility groups and state programs.
- State Medicaid agencies are necessary sources for state-specific eligibility, enrollment, renewal, and benefit details.

## 10. HealthCare.gov — Deductible glossary

Source URL: https://www.healthcare.gov/glossary/deductible/

### Usable facts

- A deductible is the amount a person pays for covered health care services before the insurance plan starts to pay.
- After meeting a deductible, the person usually pays copayments or coinsurance for covered services, and the insurer pays the rest.
- Some plans have separate deductibles for certain services, such as prescription drugs.
- Family plans may have individual and family deductibles.

### Best guide use

Use in the bill and paperwork chapter to define deductible in plain English.

## 11. HealthCare.gov — Copayment glossary

Source URL: https://www.healthcare.gov/glossary/co-payment/

### Usable facts

- A copayment is a fixed amount a person pays for a covered service after paying the deductible.
- Copayments can vary for different services within the same plan.
- A patient may owe the full allowed amount if the deductible has not been met, depending on plan rules.

### Best guide use

Use in the bill review worksheet and glossary section.

---

## 12. HealthCare.gov — Coinsurance glossary

Source URL: https://www.healthcare.gov/glossary/co-insurance/

### Usable facts

- Coinsurance is the percentage of the cost of a covered service the person pays after the deductible.
- Allowed amount matters because coinsurance is calculated against the plan-recognized amount, not simply the provider's sticker price.
- Out-of-pocket maximum rules can limit covered in-network cost exposure for some plan types.

### Best guide use

Use in the chapter explaining why insurance or Medicare can pay part of a bill while the patient still owes money.

---

## 13. HealthCare.gov — Out-of-pocket maximum glossary

Source URL: https://www.healthcare.gov/glossary/out-of-pocket-maximum-limit/

### Usable facts

- The out-of-pocket maximum is the most a person has to pay for covered services in a plan year.
- After the limit is reached, the health plan pays 100% of covered benefits for the rest of the plan year.
- Premiums, non-covered services, out-of-network care, and charges above the allowed amount may not count toward the limit.

### Best guide use

Use to explain why maximum-out-of-pocket protections are not the same as every health-related expense being capped.

---

## 14. HealthCare.gov — Allowed amount glossary

Source URL: https://www.healthcare.gov/glossary/allowed-amount/

### Usable facts

- Allowed amount is the maximum amount a plan will pay for a covered health care service.
- It may also be called eligible expense, payment allowance, or negotiated rate.
- If a provider charges more than the allowed amount, the patient may owe the difference in some situations.

### Best guide use

Use in bill review, EOB, Medicare Summary Notice, and balance-billing explanations.

## 15. SHIP Help — Local Medicare counseling

Source URL: https://www.shiphelp.org/

### Usable facts

- SHIP provides local, one-on-one insurance counseling and assistance with Medicare.
- SHIP is positioned as a trusted, unbiased source for Medicare help.
- The site helps users find a local SHIP program by state or territory.

### Best guide use

Use in every chapter where the reader needs human help comparing Medicare options, understanding plan rules, appeals, or confusing coverage questions.

## Required manuscript rules from this binder

1. Do not copy official source wording.
2. Recheck all dollar amounts and year-specific facts before final PDF publication.
3. Separate Medicare coverage from Medicaid eligibility.
4. Separate skilled care from custodial care.
5. Explain that needing care does not automatically mean that Medicare pays for it.
6. Explain that a patient can owe money even when Medicare or a plan pays part of a claim.
7. State that Medicaid rules vary by state.
8. Push readers toward official verification: Medicare, Medicaid, the plan, the facility, SHIP, billing office, or qualified professionals.
9. Avoid plan rankings, insurer recommendations, personalized eligibility determinations, legal advice, tax advice, and medical advice.
