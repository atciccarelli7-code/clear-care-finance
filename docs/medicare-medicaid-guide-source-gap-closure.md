# 1. Medicare Summary Notices

Official source:

- Medicare.gov: Medicare Summary Notice  
  `https://www.medicare.gov/basics/forms-publications-mailings/mailings/costs-and-coverage/medicare-summary-notice`

## Usable facts

- A Medicare Summary Notice is not a bill.
- People with Original Medicare receive an MSN for Part A and Part B-covered services.
- The MSN shows services and supplies billed to Medicare during a period.
- The MSN shows what Medicare paid.
- The MSN shows the maximum amount the person may owe the provider.
- People with claims during a period generally receive MSNs by mail on a periodic basis.
- Electronic MSNs can be available more frequently when claims are processed.
- A family should compare provider bills, receipts, and other insurance paperwork against the MSN before paying a confusing balance.
- If Medicare denies payment and the family disagrees, the Medicare.gov MSN page directs readers to appeal information.
- The MSN itself includes appeal instructions.

## Guide use

Use this in:

- Chapter 8: Why Medicare Can Pay and You Still Owe
- Chapter 18: Bills, EOBs, Medicare Summary Notices, and Scripts
- Bill review worksheet
- EOB-to-bill match checker copy

## Wording guardrail

Do not say an MSN proves the bill is correct. Say it is one of the key documents to compare against the provider bill before paying.

# 2. Medicare appeals

Official source:

- Medicare.gov: How do I file an appeal?  
  `https://www.medicare.gov/providers-services/claims-appeals-complaints/appeals`

## Usable facts

- A person can file an appeal if they disagree with a coverage or payment decision by Original Medicare, a Medicare Advantage plan, another Medicare health plan, or a Medicare drug plan.
- A person may appeal if Medicare or the plan refuses to cover or pay for an item, service, or drug.
- A person may appeal if Medicare or the plan changes the amount it will pay.
- A person may appeal if Medicare or the plan stops paying for an item, service, or drug the person believes is still needed.
- Medicare appeals generally have multiple levels.
- Decision letters explain how to move to the next appeal level.
- People in Medicare Advantage or drug plans should check plan materials or contact the plan for plan-specific appeal instructions.
- Plans must give written information about how to appeal.
- SHIP can provide free, personalized Medicare counseling.
- A person may have fast appeal rights when Medicare-covered hospital, skilled nursing facility, home health, comprehensive outpatient rehabilitation facility, or hospice services are ending too soon.

## Guide use

Use this in:

- Chapter 10: What to Ask Before Discharge
- Chapter 11: Rehab After a Hospital Stay
- Chapter 17: Medicare Advantage Prior Authorization
- Chapter 18: Bills, EOBs, Medicare Summary Notices, and Scripts
- Prior authorization tracker

## Wording guardrail

Do not promise that an appeal will succeed. Say that families should ask for the written denial or notice, check deadlines, and follow the appeal instructions from Medicare or the plan.

# 3. Medicare Advantage prior authorization and appeals

Official sources:

- Medicare.gov: Compare Original Medicare and Medicare Advantage  
  `https://www.medicare.gov/basics/get-started-with-medicare/get-more-coverage/your-coverage-options/compare-original-medicare-medicare-advantage`

- Medicare.gov: Your health plan options  
  `https://www.medicare.gov/health-drug-plans/health-plans/your-health-plan-options`

- Medicare.gov: How do I file an appeal?  
  `https://www.medicare.gov/providers-services/claims-appeals-complaints/appeals`

- CMS.gov: Medicare Managed Care Appeals and Grievances  
  `https://www.cms.gov/medicare/appeals-grievances/managed-care`

## Usable facts

- Medicare Advantage is another way to receive Medicare coverage through Medicare-approved private companies that must follow Medicare rules.
- Medicare Advantage plans may use networks and plan-specific rules.
- Medicare Advantage plans may require prior authorization for some services or supplies.
- Medicare Advantage plans must provide appeal information when they make a coverage or payment decision the enrollee disagrees with.
- CMS maintains Medicare Advantage grievance, organization determination, and appeals guidance.
- CMS managed care appeals guidance is the source to use for final endnote support around Medicare Advantage organization determinations, appeals, and grievance process language.

## Guide use

Use this in:

- Chapter 6: Original Medicare vs Medicare Advantage
- Chapter 10: What to Ask Before Discharge
- Chapter 11: Rehab After a Hospital Stay
- Chapter 17: Medicare Advantage Prior Authorization
- Prior authorization tracker

## Wording guardrail

Use neutral language. Avoid anti-plan framing. The guide should say:

> A clinician recommendation and plan approval are separate steps. Ask whether prior authorization is required, whether it has been submitted, whether it is approved or denied, and what the appeal deadline is if denied.

Do not say all Medicare Advantage plans deny care, delay care, waive the same rules, or handle authorization the same way.

# 4. Observation notices / inpatient vs observation rights

Official sources:

- Medicare.gov: Skilled nursing facility care  
  `https://www.medicare.gov/coverage/skilled-nursing-facility-care`

- CMS.gov: Medicare Outpatient Observation Notice / Beneficiary Notices Initiative  
  `https://www.cms.gov/medicare/forms-notices/beneficiary-notices-initiative/moon`

## Usable facts

- For Original Medicare skilled nursing facility coverage, Medicare.gov explains that observation or emergency room time before inpatient admission does not count toward the qualifying 3-day inpatient hospital stay, even if the patient was in the hospital overnight.
- Families should ask the hospital whether the patient is inpatient, outpatient, or under observation.
- CMS maintains the Medicare Outpatient Observation Notice source for standardized observation notice materials.
- The final manuscript should use CMS MOON source language to explain when the notice is required and what the notice is meant to communicate.

## Guide use

Use this in:

- Chapter 9: Inpatient vs Observation
- Chapter 10: What to Ask Before Discharge
- Chapter 11: Rehab After a Hospital Stay
- Two-page hospital handout

## Wording guardrail

Do not imply observation status affects every Medicare service in the same way. Tie the explanation to the specific rule being discussed, especially SNF coverage under Original Medicare.

# 5. Dual eligibility

Official sources:

- CMS.gov: Medicare-Medicaid Coordination Office  
  `https://www.cms.gov/medicare/medicaid-coordination/about`

- Medicaid.gov: Seniors & Medicare and Medicaid Enrollees  
  `https://www.medicaid.gov/medicaid/eligibility-policy/seniors-medicare-and-medicaid-enrollees`

- CMS.gov: Coordination of Benefits  
  `https://www.cms.gov/medicare/coordination-benefits-recovery/overview/coordination-benefits`

## Usable facts

- People enrolled in both Medicare and Medicaid are commonly described as dually eligible or Medicare-Medicaid enrollees.
- CMS has a Medicare-Medicaid Coordination Office focused on people enrolled in both programs.
- Medicaid.gov describes millions of low-income seniors and people with disabilities who are enrolled in Medicare and also receive Medicaid coverage.
- Medicaid.gov explains that Medicare enrollees with limited income and resources may receive help through Medicaid or Medicare Savings Programs.
- For services covered by both Medicare and Medicaid, Medicare generally pays first, and Medicaid may help with remaining covered amounts up to state payment limits.
- CMS coordination-of-benefits materials explain the general concept of identifying which payer has primary payment responsibility.

## Guide use

Use this in:

- Chapter 4: Medicare vs Medicaid
- Chapter 15: Long-Term Care and Medicaid
- Chapter 16: Dual Eligibility
- Medicaid/LTSS next-step worksheet

## Wording guardrail

Do not say dual eligibility means everything is free or automatically covered. Say that provider participation, state Medicaid rules, plan structure, coverage category, and service type still matter.

# 6. Medicare Savings Programs and limited vs full Medicaid help

Official sources:

- Medicare.gov: Medicare Savings Programs  
  `https://www.medicare.gov/basics/costs/help/medicare-savings-programs`

- CMS.gov: Qualified Medicare Beneficiary Program  
  `https://www.cms.gov/medicare/medicaid-coordination/about/qualified-medicare-beneficiary-program`

- Medicaid.gov: Seniors & Medicare and Medicaid Enrollees  
  `https://www.medicaid.gov/medicaid/eligibility-policy/seniors-medicare-and-medicaid-enrollees`

## Usable facts

- Medicare Savings Programs can help eligible people with Medicare costs.
- A state Medicaid program decides whether a person qualifies for an MSP.
- MSPs may help pay Medicare Part A and/or Part B premiums, and some programs may help with deductibles, copayments, and coinsurance.
- The main MSP categories include QMB, SLMB, QI, and QDWI.
- QMB can help with Medicare premiums and Medicare cost-sharing for Medicare-covered services.
- CMS explains that federal law generally prohibits Medicare providers and suppliers from billing QMB individuals for Medicare Part A and Part B cost-sharing for Medicare-covered items and services.
- SLMB and QI are more limited than QMB and focus on Part B premium help.
- QDWI is a specific program related to Part A premium help for certain disabled and working individuals.
- Medicaid.gov also explains that some Medicare enrollees with limited income and resources may receive Medicaid help with premiums and out-of-pocket costs.

## Guide use

Use this in:

- Chapter 4: Medicare vs Medicaid
- Chapter 8: Why Medicare Can Pay and You Still Owe
- Chapter 16: Dual Eligibility
- Bill review worksheet

## Wording guardrail

Use “may help” unless discussing QMB billing protections with precise CMS-supported language. Do not tell a reader they qualify. Direct them to the state Medicaid agency or Medicare.gov MSP application guidance.

# 7. Medicaid estate recovery

Official source:

- Medicaid.gov: Estate Recovery  
  `https://www.medicaid.gov/medicaid/eligibility-policy/estate-recovery`

## Usable facts

- States are required to seek recovery from estates for certain Medicaid benefits.
- For individuals age 55 or older, states must seek recovery for nursing facility services, home and community-based services, and related hospital and prescription drug services.
- States may have options to recover additional Medicaid payments, subject to federal and state rules.
- Medicaid.gov identifies limits and protections, including situations involving a surviving spouse, a child under age 21, or a child of any age who is blind or disabled.
- States must establish procedures for undue hardship waivers.
- Estate recovery is state-specific and legally sensitive.

## Include in guide?

Include only as a brief caution or appendix item if the guide discusses long-term care and Medicaid planning questions.

Recommended wording:

> Medicaid can have estate recovery rules. These rules are state-specific and legally sensitive. Families should verify with the state Medicaid agency and consider qualified legal guidance before making decisions.

## Wording guardrail

Do not provide asset-protection strategies, Medicaid planning tactics, or advice on how to avoid estate recovery.

# 8. Spousal impoverishment

Official source:

- Medicaid.gov: Spousal Impoverishment  
  `https://www.medicaid.gov/medicaid/eligibility-policy/spousal-impoverishment`

## Usable facts

- Federal spousal impoverishment rules were created to help protect a spouse who remains in the community when the other spouse needs certain long-term care.
- Medicaid.gov explains that certain income and resource protections may apply to the community spouse.
- Medicaid.gov posts annual spousal impoverishment standards.
- Post-eligibility treatment of income rules may determine how much income an institutionalized person must contribute toward care after Medicaid eligibility is established.
- These rules are state-specific, technical, and legally sensitive.

## Include in guide?

Include only as a short “ask about this” box, not a planning section.

Recommended wording:

> If one spouse needs long-term care and the other spouse still lives in the community, ask the state Medicaid agency or a qualified elder-law attorney about spousal impoverishment protections. Do not assume all household income or assets are treated the same way in every state.

## Wording guardrail

Do not calculate eligibility, give legal advice, recommend transfers, or imply that a spouse can always keep a specific amount.

# 9. State Medicaid and LTSS verification language

Official sources:

- Medicaid.gov: Eligibility Policy  
  `https://www.medicaid.gov/medicaid/eligibility-policy`

- Medicaid.gov: Long-Term Services and Supports  
  `https://www.medicaid.gov/medicaid/long-term-services-supports`

- State Medicaid agency pages for any state-specific appendix.

## Usable facts

- Medicaid is administered by states within federal rules.
- States must cover certain mandatory eligibility groups and may cover optional groups.
- Eligibility rules vary by group and state.
- Medicaid LTSS can include institutional care and home- and community-based services.
- State Medicaid agencies are required sources for state-specific eligibility, enrollment, renewal, benefit, waiver, estate recovery, and LTSS details.
- Medicaid beneficiaries generally must be residents of the state providing Medicaid.
- Medicaid has appeal/fair-hearing processes, but state structures can vary.

## Guide use

- Chapter 4: Medicare vs Medicaid
- Chapter 15: Long-Term Care and Medicaid
- Chapter 16: Dual Eligibility
- Medicaid/LTSS next-step worksheet
- Any future state appendix

## Wording guardrail
