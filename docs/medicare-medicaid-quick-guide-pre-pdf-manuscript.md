# Page 1 — Start Here

## Direct answer

Use this quick guide when a patient, spouse, parent, or loved one is in the hospital, leaving the hospital, being offered rehab, needing home support, facing long-term care, or receiving a confusing bill.

## Plain-English explanation

The goal is not to decide coverage. The goal is to slow the situation down and help the family ask better questions before agreeing to a plan, signing paperwork, choosing a facility, or paying a bill.

A good first question is not "Does Medicare cover this?" A better first question is: which payer, plan, provider, facility, document, rule, and cost-sharing amount applies?

## Use this when

- Discharge is being discussed.
- Rehab or a skilled nursing facility is recommended.
- Home health, oxygen, walker, hospital bed, or other equipment is ordered.
- A family is worried about long-term care.
- A Medicare Summary Notice, Explanation of Benefits, denial, or bill does not make sense.

## Source note

Orientation page. Use Medicare.gov, Medicaid.gov, CMS.gov, official state Medicaid agencies, SHIP, plan documents, and facility or billing-office documents for situation-specific verification.

# Page 2 — The 5 Misunderstandings

## Direct answer

Most family confusion comes from five misunderstandings: Medicare and Medicaid are different; covered does not always mean free; skilled care is different from custodial care; an overnight hospital stay does not always mean inpatient status; and Medicare Advantage may involve networks, referrals, or prior authorization.

## Plain-English explanation

A person can truly need care and still run into coverage rules. A clinician can recommend care and the plan may still need documentation or approval. Medicare can pay its share and the patient may still owe a deductible, copay, coinsurance, daily amount, or non-covered charge.

## Questions to ask

- Is this Medicare, Medicaid, both, or another plan?
- Is this skilled medical care or daily living help?
- Is the patient inpatient, outpatient, or observation?
- Is the provider or facility in-network or Medicare-certified?
- Is prior authorization required?
- What could the patient owe?

## Source note

Supported by Medicare.gov coverage, cost, skilled nursing facility, long-term care, and Medicare Advantage comparison guidance.

# Page 3 — Medicare vs Medicaid

## Direct answer

Medicare is federal health insurance mainly tied to age, disability, or certain conditions. Medicaid is a joint federal-state program, run by states within federal rules, that helps eligible people with health coverage and may help with long-term services and supports.

## Plain-English explanation

Medicare asks whether the person qualifies for federal health insurance and whether the service fits a Medicare benefit and coverage rule.

Medicaid asks whether the person meets state rules for assistance. Those rules can involve income, resources, disability, family status, medical need, age, pregnancy, children, or other eligibility categories.

Some people have both programs. That can reduce costs, but it does not mean every service, provider, facility, or bill is automatically covered.

## Questions to ask

- Does the patient have Medicare, Medicaid, both, or neither?
- Is the service medical care, skilled care, custodial care, or long-term support?
- Which state Medicaid agency applies?
- Does the provider accept the relevant coverage?
- Is the patient receiving full Medicaid or limited help with Medicare costs?

## Source note

Supported by Medicare.gov, Medicaid.gov eligibility policy, Medicaid.gov long-term services and supports, CMS Medicare-Medicaid coordination resources, and Medicaid.gov Medicare-Medicaid enrollee resources.

# Page 4 — Original Medicare vs Medicare Advantage

## Direct answer

Original Medicare and Medicare Advantage are two different ways to receive Medicare coverage. Original Medicare generally lets a person use any doctor or hospital that takes Medicare. Medicare Advantage plans may require in-network providers for non-emergency care and may require referrals or prior authorization for certain services or supplies.

## Plain-English explanation

Original Medicare includes Part A and Part B. A person may also have Part D drug coverage, Medigap, Medicaid, retiree coverage, or another supplement.

Medicare Advantage is offered by Medicare-approved private companies. These plans must cover medically necessary services Original Medicare covers, but they can use plan rules such as networks, service areas, referrals, and prior authorization for certain services or supplies.

## Questions to ask

- Is this Original Medicare or Medicare Advantage?
- Is the provider, facility, pharmacy, or equipment supplier in-network or approved?
- Is prior authorization required?
- What is the maximum out-of-pocket limit for covered Medicare services?
- Does the patient also have Part D, Medigap, Medicaid, retiree coverage, or another supplement?

## Source note

Supported by Medicare.gov How Medicare Works, Medicare.gov Compare Original Medicare and Medicare Advantage, Medicare.gov Costs, and CMS Medicare managed care appeals guidance.

# Page 5 — Inpatient vs Observation

## Direct answer

A patient can stay overnight in a hospital and still not be formally admitted as an inpatient. For Original Medicare skilled nursing facility coverage, observation or emergency room time before inpatient admission does not count toward the qualifying 3-day inpatient hospital stay, even if the patient was in the hospital overnight.

## Plain-English explanation

Hospital status is not only about where the patient sleeps. It is a billing and coverage classification. For some Medicare rules, especially skilled nursing facility coverage under Original Medicare, inpatient status and timing can matter.

Families should ask the hospital directly what the patient's current status is and when inpatient admission began, if it began.

## Questions to ask

- What is the patient's official hospital status right now?
- Is the patient inpatient, outpatient, or observation?
- When did inpatient admission begin, if it began?
- Does this status affect rehab or skilled nursing facility coverage?
- Has the hospital provided any required observation notice?
- How does the Medicare Advantage plan handle this, if applicable?

## Source note

Supported by Medicare.gov Skilled Nursing Facility Care and CMS Medicare Outpatient Observation Notice / Beneficiary Notices Initiative. Exact CMS MOON wording and timing should be rechecked before final public release.

# Page 6 — Before Discharge

## Direct answer

Before discharge, separate the care plan from the payment plan. The care team may recommend a setting or service, but the payer may still require documentation, authorization, provider participation, or written notices.

## Plain-English explanation

Discharge can move quickly. Families may hear words like rehab, home health, walker, oxygen, therapy, skilled nursing, prior authorization, or facility placement without knowing what is approved or what the patient may owe.

Do not wait until after transfer or discharge to ask the cost and coverage questions.

## Questions to ask before agreeing

- Where is the patient going after discharge?
- What skilled need is documented?
- What services, equipment, medications, and follow-up visits are ordered?
- Which payer is expected to pay?
- Is authorization required and approved?
- What could the patient owe?
- What happens if coverage is denied or ends?
- What written notice or appeal instructions should we review?
- What is the backup plan if home is not safe?

## Source note

Supported by Medicare.gov skilled nursing facility, home health, durable medical equipment, costs, appeals, Medicare Advantage comparison, and CMS observation-notice resources.

# Page 7 — Rehab and Skilled Nursing Facility Care

## Direct answer

Medicare may cover short-term skilled rehab or skilled nursing facility care when rules are met, but coverage is not automatic. The patient usually needs skilled care, the facility must meet requirements, and plan rules may apply.

## Plain-English explanation

Rehab is where families often discover the difference between needing help and meeting coverage rules. A recommendation from a clinician, therapist, or hospital does not guarantee payment.

Original Medicare has specific skilled nursing facility requirements, including a qualifying inpatient hospital stay unless an allowed exception or waiver applies. Medicare Advantage plans may use network rules, authorization rules, and plan-specific processes.

## Questions to ask

- What skilled service is being ordered?
- Is the recommended setting SNF, inpatient rehab, home health, or outpatient therapy?
- Is the facility Medicare-certified or in-network?
- Is prior authorization required?
- Has authorization been approved?
- What cost-sharing might apply?
- What happens if the patient still needs help after skilled care ends?
- If denied or ending, what is the appeal deadline?

## Source note

Supported by Medicare.gov Skilled Nursing Facility Care, Medicare.gov Costs, Medicare.gov Compare Original Medicare and Medicare Advantage, and Medicare.gov appeals guidance. Current-year dollar amounts are intentionally not listed in this quick guide; verify them before release and use the full guide or site calculator for updated figures.

# Page 8 — Home Health, Equipment, and Long-Term Care

## Direct answer

Medicare may cover certain home health services and durable medical equipment when rules are met. It generally does not pay for 24-hour home care, meal delivery, unrelated homemaker services, or custodial care when that is the only care needed.

## Plain-English explanation

Home health is not the same as having a full-time aide at home. Medicare-covered home health generally requires part-time or intermittent skilled services, a provider order, homebound status, and a Medicare-certified home health agency.

Long-term custodial care is different. Help with bathing, dressing, toileting, meals, supervision, transportation, and daily living may be very necessary, but Medicare generally does not pay for most long-term custodial care.

## Questions to ask

- Is the need skilled care, home health, equipment, custodial care, or long-term support?
- Is the agency Medicare-certified or plan-approved?
- Is the equipment supplier enrolled in Medicare or approved by the plan?
- Does the supplier accept assignment?
- Should Medicaid, LTSS, state programs, community supports, or professional guidance be explored?

## Source note

Supported by Medicare.gov Home Health Services, Medicare.gov Durable Medical Equipment Coverage, Medicare.gov Long-Term Care, Medicaid.gov LTSS, and Medicaid.gov eligibility policy.

# Page 9 — Bills, EOBs, and Medicare Summary Notices

## Direct answer

Before paying a confusing bill, compare the provider bill to the Medicare Summary Notice, Explanation of Benefits, or plan paperwork. Confirm the date of service, provider, allowed amount, plan payment, denial reason, and patient responsibility.

## Plain-English explanation

One hospital visit can create multiple bills. A facility may bill separately from physicians, radiology, lab, ambulance, anesthesia, therapy, or outside professionals.

For Original Medicare, the Medicare Summary Notice is not a bill. It shows what was billed to Medicare, what Medicare paid, and the maximum amount the person may owe. For Medicare Advantage, Part D, or other plans, the EOB or plan paperwork may serve a similar comparison function.

## Questions to ask before paying

- What date of service is this?
- Which provider or facility is billing me?
- Has Medicare or the plan processed this claim?
- Does the bill match the MSN, EOB, or plan paperwork?
- What was the allowed amount?
- What did the plan pay?
- Why is this listed as patient responsibility?
- Is financial assistance available?
- Should this be appealed, corrected, or rebilled?

## Source note

Supported by Medicare.gov Medicare Summary Notice, Medicare.gov appeals, Medicare.gov Costs, and HealthCare.gov glossary sources for deductible, copayment, coinsurance, allowed amount, and out-of-pocket maximum.

# Page 10 — Scripts and Next Steps

## Direct answer

Use the quick guide to ask better first questions. Use the full Hospital Family Guide when the family needs deeper explanations, worksheets, source notes, and tool links.

## Phone scripts

- Hospital/case management: "Can you tell me the patient's current status, the recommended discharge setting, what skilled need is documented, and which payer rule applies?"
- Facility or home health agency: "Are you Medicare-certified, in-network, or approved by the plan, and what could the patient owe?"
- Medicare Advantage plan: "Is prior authorization required, has it been submitted, and is it approved, denied, pending, or partially approved?"
- Billing office: "Can you compare this bill against the MSN, EOB, or plan paperwork and explain why this amount is patient responsibility?"
