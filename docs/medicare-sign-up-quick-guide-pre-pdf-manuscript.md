# Page 1 — Start Here

## Direct answer

Medicare sign-up starts with one question: do you need to sign up now, or will you automatically get Part A and Part B?

## First check

- **Age:** Are you turning 65 soon, already 65, or under 65 because of disability or another qualifying condition?
- **Current coverage:** Do you have job-based coverage, spouse coverage, retiree coverage, COBRA, Marketplace coverage, VA/TRICARE, Medicaid, or no coverage?
- **Work status:** Are you or your spouse still actively working for the employer that provides the coverage?
- **Part A/B status:** Do you already have Part A, Part B, both, or neither?
- **Deadline:** What is the enrollment window or special enrollment period?

## First move

Use Medicare.gov's sign-up tools and Social Security's Medicare application path before assuming the timing.

# Page 2 — The 5 Things to Know Before Signing Up

## Direct answer

Medicare sign-up is not just a birthday task. Timing, employer coverage, penalties, HSA rules, and next coverage decisions can all matter.

## Watch out

- **Initial Enrollment Period:** Most people first get a 7-month window around turning 65.
- **Automatic enrollment:** Some people automatically get Part A and Part B; others need to sign up.
- **Employer coverage:** Active job-based coverage can change the timing question.
- **Late penalties:** Waiting without a valid reason can create coverage gaps or penalties.
- **Next decisions:** Part A and Part B are not the same as choosing Medicare Advantage, Part D, or Medigap.

## Better question

Ask: “What is my current coverage, what enrollment period applies, and what happens if I wait?”

# Page 3 — Know the Parts Before You Enroll

## Direct answer

Part A and Part B are the starting point for Original Medicare. Other choices may come after that.

## Compare

- **Part A:** Hospital insurance.
- **Part B:** Medical insurance.
- **Part D:** Drug coverage that may be added separately depending on the Medicare path.
- **Medicare Advantage:** A private plan approved by Medicare that generally requires Part A and Part B.
- **Medigap:** Supplemental coverage that works with Original Medicare, not Medicare Advantage.

## Ask

- Am I signing up for Part A, Part B, or both?
- Do I need drug coverage after that?
- Am I comparing Original Medicare plus add-ons versus Medicare Advantage?

# Page 4 — Your First Enrollment Window

## Direct answer

For many people, the first Medicare sign-up window starts 3 months before the month they turn 65 and ends 3 months after that birthday month.

## Timeline

- **3 months before:** Start checking whether to sign up.
- **Birthday month:** Coverage timing depends on when you sign up.
- **3 months after:** The first window closes.
- **After the window:** You may need a General Enrollment Period or Special Enrollment Period.

## Ask

- What month do I turn 65?
- What date would coverage start if I sign up now?
- What happens if I wait?

# Page 5 — Still Working at 65

## Direct answer

If you or your spouse still has active job-based coverage, do not guess. Ask Medicare, Social Security, and the employer benefits office how that coverage works with Medicare.

## Verify

- **Employer size:** Medicare rules can treat employer coverage differently depending on employer size and disability status.
- **Active employment:** Coverage from current work is different from COBRA or retiree coverage.
- **Part B timing:** Some people can delay Part B; others may face gaps or penalties.
- **HSA issue:** Medicare enrollment can affect Health Savings Account contribution eligibility.

## Ask HR or benefits

- Is this coverage based on current active employment?
- How does the plan coordinate with Medicare after age 65?
- Do I need Part A, Part B, both, or neither right now?
- What documentation should I keep for Social Security?

# Page 6 — Coverage That Can Confuse the Timing

## Direct answer

COBRA, retiree coverage, Marketplace coverage, VA, CHAMPVA, TRICARE, Medicaid, and employer coverage are not interchangeable for Medicare sign-up timing.

## Sort the coverage

- **Current employer coverage:** May support a Special Enrollment Period in some cases.
- **COBRA:** Does not extend the same Medicare Part B Special Enrollment Period in the same way as active employer coverage.
- **Retiree coverage:** Not the same as coverage from current work.
- **Marketplace coverage:** Not the same as active employer coverage.
- **Medicaid:** May create separate timing and coordination questions.
- **VA/TRICARE/CHAMPVA:** Verify directly because coordination rules can be specific.

## Ask

- What type of coverage do I have right now?
- Is it based on current work?
- Does Medicare.gov say this coverage changes when I should sign up?

# Page 7 — How to Actually Sign Up

## Direct answer

Medicare.gov sends people to official sign-up paths and forms. Many people sign up through Social Security.

## Action path

- **Check timing:** Use Medicare.gov's sign-up guidance before applying.
- **Apply:** Use the official Social Security Medicare sign-up process when appropriate.
- **Forms:** If you already have Part A and need Part B, CMS and Social Security forms may apply.
- **Keep proof:** Save confirmation numbers, forms, employer documents, and notices.

## Ask

- Am I applying online, by phone, by mail, or through a local Social Security office?
- Do I need employer coverage proof?
- What date will my coverage start?

# Page 8 — After Part A and Part B

## Direct answer

Getting Part A and Part B is not the end of Medicare decisions. It often starts the next set of choices.

## Next choices

- **Original Medicare:** Usually Part A and Part B, with possible Part D and Medigap decisions.
- **Medicare Advantage:** A private plan path that generally requires Part A and Part B.
- **Drug coverage:** Decide whether and how prescription drug coverage fits.
- **Doctors and hospitals:** Check provider access before choosing a plan path.
- **Costs:** Premiums, deductibles, copays, coinsurance, and out-of-pocket limits can differ by path.

## Ask before choosing

- Which doctors, hospitals, medications, and pharmacies matter most?
- What is the worst-case annual cost exposure?
- What is the appeal or authorization process?

# Page 9 — Mistakes to Avoid

## Direct answer

Most Medicare sign-up mistakes come from assuming one type of coverage counts like another, ignoring deadlines, or choosing a plan before checking doctors, drugs, and costs.

## Avoid

- **Assuming automatic enrollment:** Confirm whether you will automatically get Part A and Part B.
- **Missing the first window:** Know your Initial Enrollment Period.
- **Confusing COBRA with active employer coverage:** Verify the rule before relying on it.
- **Ignoring HSA timing:** Ask before contributing to an HSA after Medicare starts.
- **Choosing by premium only:** Check doctors, drugs, hospitals, authorizations, and out-of-pocket exposure.

## Better question

Ask: “What decision am I actually making today: enroll in Part A/B, choose a coverage path, pick drug coverage, or compare plans?”

# Page 10 — Scripts and Next Steps

## Direct answer

When you are unsure, use short questions and get the answer from the official source, employer benefits office, SHIP counselor, Social Security, or plan.

## Scripts

- “Do I need to sign up now, or will I be automatically enrolled?”
- “What is my Initial Enrollment Period?”
- “Does my current coverage let me delay Part B without a penalty?”
- “Is this coverage from active employment, COBRA, retiree coverage, Marketplace coverage, Medicaid, VA, CHAMPVA, or TRICARE?”
- “What date would Part A and Part B start?”
- “What proof should I keep?”
- “Who can verify this in writing?”

## Next step

Use Medicare.gov's sign-up tools, Social Security's Medicare application process, and SHIP counseling resources before making an enrollment decision.
