# Page 1 — Do I need to sign up for Medicare now?

## Direct answer

Start with one question: are you already getting Medicare automatically, or do you need to sign up? Do not assume Medicare starts perfectly just because someone turns 65. The answer can depend on Social Security benefits, current work coverage, spouse coverage, disability status, and timing.

## Why this matters

Medicare enrollment mistakes are easy to make because different people have different starting points. Some people are automatically enrolled. Others must apply. Some people can delay Part B because of active employer coverage. Others may create a coverage gap or late penalty if they wait. Before choosing a plan, drug coverage, or supplement, first confirm whether Part A and Part B need action.

## What to ask

- **Automatic or manual:** “Will I automatically get Part A and Part B, or do I need to sign up?”
- **Timing:** “What enrollment period applies to me?”
- **Coverage today:** “Do I have active employer coverage, spouse coverage, retiree coverage, COBRA, Marketplace coverage, Medicaid, VA, TRICARE, or no coverage?”
- **Work status:** “Am I or my spouse still actively working for the employer that provides the coverage?”
- **Start date:** “If I sign up now, when would coverage begin?”

## What to keep

- **Coverage proof:** employer benefit letters, plan cards, COBRA/retiree notices, Marketplace notices, or Medicaid notices.
- **Medicare proof:** Part A/Part B start dates, Social Security confirmation, application confirmation, and notices.
- **Call log:** date, agency, representative, reference number, and exact next step.

## Short script

“I am turning 65 and need to know whether I should sign up now or wait. My current coverage is [coverage type]. Can you tell me what enrollment period applies, what coverage would start when, and what proof I should keep?”

# Page 2 — What are Part A and Part B?

## Direct answer

Part A and Part B are the starting point for many Medicare decisions. Part A is generally hospital insurance. Part B is generally medical insurance. Other choices, such as Medicare Advantage, Part D, or Medigap, usually come after understanding Part A and Part B.

## Why this matters

People often say “Medicare” as if it is one thing, but the actual decision may be about one part of Medicare. Signing up for Part A is not always the same as signing up for Part B. Having Part A and Part B is not the same as having drug coverage. Choosing Medicare Advantage is not the same as choosing Original Medicare with a Medigap policy. Sorting the parts prevents the wrong conversation.

## What to ask

- **Part A:** “Do I already have Part A, and when did or will it start?”
- **Part B:** “Do I need Part B now, or can I delay it based on valid active employer coverage?”
- **Drug coverage:** “Do I need Part D or another source of creditable drug coverage?”
- **Coverage path:** “Am I comparing Original Medicare, Medicare Advantage, Medigap, or Part D?”
- **Costs:** “What premium, deductible, copay, coinsurance, or out-of-pocket exposure applies?”

## What to keep

- **Medicare card or letter:** Part A and Part B effective dates.
- **Coverage comparisons:** notes on Original Medicare, Medicare Advantage, Part D, and Medigap.
- **Drug coverage proof:** plan notice if drug coverage is creditable.

## Short script

“I want to make sure I am using the right words. Am I making a Part A decision, a Part B decision, a drug coverage decision, or a plan-choice decision?”

# Page 3 — What is my first Medicare enrollment window?

## Direct answer

For many people, the first Medicare sign-up window starts three months before the month they turn 65 and ends three months after that birthday month. Coverage start dates can depend on when the person signs up.

## Why this matters

Missing the first window can create confusion, delays, or penalties unless another valid enrollment path applies. This is where people often get tripped up: they know they are turning 65, but they do not know the exact window, the coverage start date, or whether current coverage changes the answer.

## What to ask

- **Birthday month:** “What month do I turn 65?”
- **Window:** “What are the first and last dates of my Initial Enrollment Period?”
- **Start date:** “If I sign up this month, when would Part A and Part B start?”
- **Delay question:** “Is there a valid reason for me to delay Part B?”
- **Penalty question:** “Could waiting create a late enrollment penalty or coverage gap?”

## What to keep

- **Calendar:** first and last date of the enrollment window.
- **Confirmation:** Social Security or Medicare.gov application confirmation.
- **Written answer:** note from benefits office, SHIP, Social Security, Medicare.gov tool, or plan document if timing is uncertain.

## Short script

“I need to know my exact enrollment window and coverage start date. If I apply now, when would coverage begin? If I wait, what would the risk be?”

# Page 4 — What if I am still working at 65?

## Direct answer

If you or your spouse still has active job-based coverage, do not guess. Ask the employer benefits office, Medicare, Social Security, and SHIP how that coverage coordinates with Medicare before delaying Part B or changing coverage.

## Why this matters

Active employer coverage can change the Medicare timing question for some people. But not all coverage counts the same way. Coverage from current work is different from COBRA, retiree coverage, Marketplace coverage, or a plan that sounds employer-related but does not count the way someone expects. This is also where Health Savings Account contributions can become an issue.

## What to ask

- **Active employment:** “Is this coverage based on current active employment?”
- **Employer size:** “Does employer size affect whether Medicare pays first or second?”
- **Part B:** “Can I delay Part B without a penalty or gap?”
- **HSA:** “Would Medicare enrollment affect HSA contribution eligibility?”
- **Proof:** “What employer form or coverage proof should I keep?”

## What to keep

- **Employer proof:** coverage letter, benefits summary, and any required employer form.
- **HSA notes:** date Medicare would start and the date HSA contributions should stop if applicable.
- **Call log:** benefits office, Social Security, Medicare, and SHIP answers.

## Short script

“I am still working and have coverage through current employment. Can you confirm whether I can delay Part B, whether Medicare would be primary or secondary, and what proof I should keep for Social Security?”

# Page 5 — Which coverage types can confuse Medicare timing?

## Direct answer

COBRA, retiree coverage, Marketplace coverage, Medicaid, VA, CHAMPVA, TRICARE, and active employer coverage are not interchangeable. Each can affect Medicare decisions differently.

## Why this matters

A common mistake is assuming “I have insurance” means “I can safely delay Medicare.” That is not always true. The timing question usually depends on the type of coverage, whether it comes from current work, and whether Medicare.gov or Social Security treats it as valid for a Special Enrollment Period.

## What to ask

- **Coverage type:** “Exactly what coverage do I have right now?”
- **Current work:** “Is it based on active current employment?”
- **COBRA/retiree:** “Is this COBRA or retiree coverage instead of active employer coverage?”
- **Marketplace:** “Will Marketplace coverage change after Medicare eligibility?”
- **VA/TRICARE/CHAMPVA:** “Who should verify how this works with Medicare?”
- **Medicaid:** “Does Medicaid or Medicare Savings Program help apply too?”

## What to keep

- **Plan notices:** COBRA, retiree, Marketplace, VA, TRICARE, CHAMPVA, Medicaid, or employer materials.
- **Creditable coverage notices:** especially for drug coverage.
- **Written timing answer:** from the official source or benefits office.

## Short script

“My current coverage is [coverage type]. I need to know whether this lets me delay Medicare Part B or drug coverage without a penalty or gap. Who can verify that in writing?”

# Page 6 — How do I actually sign up?

## Direct answer

Many people sign up for Medicare through Social Security after checking Medicare.gov’s sign-up guidance. If someone already has Part A and needs Part B, specific forms and employer coverage proof may apply.

## Why this matters

People often jump straight to shopping for Medicare Advantage or Medigap before the basic enrollment step is clear. The first job is to confirm whether the person needs Part A, Part B, both, neither, or proof for a Special Enrollment Period. The application path should match the enrollment situation.

## What to ask

- **Path:** “Should I apply online, by phone, by mail, or through a Social Security office?”
- **Forms:** “Do I need a Part B enrollment form or employer coverage form?”
- **Effective date:** “What date will coverage start?”
- **Proof:** “What confirmation number, receipt, or mailed notice should I save?”
- **Correction:** “Who do I call if the start date or coverage looks wrong?”

## What to keep

- **Application proof:** confirmation number, submitted forms, screenshots, mailed copies, or fax/upload receipts.
- **Employer forms:** employer coverage verification if used.
- **Medicare notice:** effective dates and any follow-up instructions.

## Short script

“I believe I need to sign up for [Part A / Part B / both]. Which official application path applies, what forms are required, and when will coverage start?”

Built from Medicare.gov sign-up guidance, Social Security Medicare application guidance, CMS Part A/Part B form references, and SHIP resources.

# Page 7 — What happens after Part A and Part B?

## Direct answer

Getting Part A and Part B is not the end of Medicare decisions. It usually starts the next decision: Original Medicare with possible Part D and Medigap, or Medicare Advantage.

## Why this matters

The best path depends on doctors, hospitals, medications, pharmacies, travel, costs, authorization rules, network preferences, and risk tolerance. This guide should not tell someone which plan to pick. It should help them slow down and compare the right issues before choosing.

## What to ask

- **Doctors:** “Which doctors and hospitals matter most?”
- **Drugs:** “Which medications and pharmacies need to be checked?”
- **Path:** “Am I comparing Original Medicare plus Part D/Medigap against Medicare Advantage?”
- **Costs:** “What are the premiums, deductibles, copays, coinsurance, and worst-case out-of-pocket exposure?”
- **Rules:** “Are referrals, networks, or prior authorizations important for this plan path?”

## What to keep

- **Medication list:** drug name, dose, frequency, and pharmacy.
- **Provider list:** doctors, hospitals, specialists, and preferred facilities.
- **Cost notes:** premium, deductible, out-of-pocket maximum if applicable, and drug costs.
- **Plan documents:** Summary of Benefits, Evidence of Coverage, formularies, and provider directory screenshots if used.

## Short script

“I am not choosing by premium alone. I want to compare doctors, drugs, hospitals, total cost exposure, and authorization rules before deciding.”

# Page 8 — What mistakes should I avoid?

## Direct answer

Most Medicare sign-up mistakes come from assuming, waiting without proof, confusing coverage types, ignoring drug coverage, or choosing a plan before checking doctors, medications, and costs.

## Why this matters

Medicare is full of terms that sound familiar but work differently. Someone may assume they are automatically enrolled when they are not. They may assume COBRA works like active employer coverage. They may ignore Part D because they do not take many medications. They may choose a low premium plan without checking network or drug costs. Each mistake can create practical problems later.

## What to ask

- **Automatic enrollment:** “Will I be enrolled automatically?”
- **Delay:** “What proof says I can safely wait?”
- **Drug coverage:** “Do I need creditable drug coverage?”
- **Provider access:** “Are my doctors, hospitals, medications, and pharmacies covered?”
- **Cost exposure:** “What is the worst-case annual cost?”
- **Documentation:** “What should I save if someone later questions the timing?”

## What to keep

- **Timing proof:** enrollment window, Special Enrollment Period proof, and employer coverage documents.
- **Drug proof:** creditable coverage notice or Part D selection documents.
- **Plan comparison notes:** doctors, hospitals, drugs, pharmacy, and cost review.

## Short script

“Before I wait or choose a plan, I want to confirm the rule, the deadline, the proof I need to keep, and the worst-case cost or access issue.”

# Page 9 — What should I keep in a Medicare folder?

## Direct answer

Keep anything that proves your Medicare timing, coverage decision, employer coverage, drug coverage, plan choice, and conversations with official sources.

## Why this matters

Most Medicare problems become harder when the paper trail is missing. If a coverage date looks wrong, a penalty appears, a drug is not covered, or a plan says a provider is out of network, the person with the best records has a better chance of getting a clear answer.

## What to ask

- **Enrollment:** “What document proves when I applied and when coverage starts?”
- **Employer coverage:** “What document proves I had active employer coverage?”
- **Drug coverage:** “What document proves creditable drug coverage?”
- **Plan choice:** “What plan documents should I save?”
- **Call log:** “Can you give me a reference number for this call?”

## What to keep

- **Medicare:** card, award letter, Part A/Part B effective dates, and application confirmation.
- **Employer:** benefits letter, required employer forms, and current-work coverage proof.
- **Drug coverage:** creditable coverage notice, Part D plan confirmation, or formulary notes.
- **Plan documents:** Summary of Benefits, Evidence of Coverage, provider directory, pharmacy/drug review, and cost notes.
- **Call notes:** agency, representative, date, time, reference number, and next step.

## Short script

“I am keeping a Medicare folder so I can prove timing and coverage later. What document should I save from this decision or conversation?”

# Page 10 — Who should I call, and what should I say?

## Direct answer

Use short questions and call the source that controls the answer: Medicare.gov for education and plan comparison, Social Security for many enrollment steps, employer benefits for current-work coverage, SHIP for counseling, and the plan for plan-specific rules.

## Why this matters

A vague question like “What should I do about Medicare?” can send someone in circles. A better question names the decision: sign up now or wait, Part A/B start date, employer coverage proof, drug coverage, plan comparison, penalty, or appeal. One clear question gets a clearer answer.

## What to ask

- **Medicare timing:** “What enrollment period applies to me?”
- **Social Security:** “What application or form do I need?”
- **Employer benefits:** “Is this active employer coverage, and can I delay Part B?”
- **SHIP:** “Can someone help me review the timing and coverage decision?”
- **Plan:** “Are my doctors, drugs, hospitals, and pharmacies covered, and what are the authorization rules?”

## What to keep

- **Names and numbers:** representative name, agency or plan, phone number, and reference number.
- **Deadlines:** enrollment window, form deadline, coverage start date, and plan election deadline.
- **Proof:** application confirmation, forms, employer letters, creditable coverage notices, and plan confirmations.

## Short script

“I need help with one specific Medicare question: [sign up now / delay Part B / coverage start date / employer proof / drug coverage / plan comparison]. What rule applies, what document explains it, and what should I do next?”
